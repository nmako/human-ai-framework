## Section purpose

This section establishes how the company integrates AI-related impacts on people into its existing human rights due diligence (HRDD). It is the framework's intellectual core: the argument that AI is not a separate compliance discipline parallel to HRDD but a category of risk addressed within the same methodology.

The framework's position is explicit: AI-related impacts on workers in own operations, on supplier workers in the value chain, and on end-users and affected communities downstream are human rights risks. They are identified, assessed, prevented, mitigated, remediated, tracked, and communicated using the same UNGPs methodology that companies already apply to other human rights risks. The company does not run a separate AI HRDD process; it extends its existing aktsomhetsvurderinger or equivalent HRDD process to address AI-related risks alongside modern slavery, supply chain labor rights, freedom of association, decent wages, occupational health and safety, environmental rights, and other established categories.

This is not a rhetorical claim. It changes how the work is organized operationally. A company that runs a separate AI compliance track parallel to its HRDD work produces fragmented assessments, gaps where AI risks fall between the tracks, and duplication where the same risk surfaces in both. A company that integrates AI into HRDD identifies the risk once, addresses it through one process, and communicates it through one reporting cycle.

The section anchors on UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights methodology because UNGPs is the global standard, applicable to companies in every jurisdiction whether or not binding HRDD law applies. National HRDD instruments — CSDDD, the Norwegian Transparency Act, German LkSG, French Loi de Vigilance, UK Modern Slavery Act, and others documented in Section 8 — operationalize UNGPs principles into binding law for in-scope companies. Companies outside the scope of binding HRDD law still apply UNGPs methodology voluntarily as the established global standard.

The framework's distinctive contribution, made operational in this section: applying UNGPs methodology specifically to AI-related impacts produces governance work that most current AI policy templates skip entirely. The translation between AI Act risk classification logic and HRDD impact-based logic is documented in `03_translation_layer.md` and operationalized in Section 6 Requirement 6.6.

This section covers three populations:

- **Own workforce.** AI-related HRDD impacts on the company's own workers. Cross-references Section 3 (worker rights) for the operational worker-protection commitments and Section 3 Requirement 3.5 specifically for the worker consultation requirement that applies cross-sectionally; this section provides the HRDD framing.
- **Supply chain workers.** AI-related HRDD impacts on workers in the company's supply chain — handled substantively in Section 8 (supply chain and third-party AI). Section 7 references this work without restating it.
- **Value chain more broadly.** AI-related HRDD impacts on end-users, affected communities, and others downstream of the company's operations. Reach scales by ambition tier: Tier C companies commit to addressing value chain impacts substantively; Tier B companies see these requirements as the ambition path; Tier A companies see them as voluntary alignment opportunities.

## Section narrative (Part 2 — Operational detail)

> *Suggested policy text for Part 2. The Generator produces this language as the opening of the operational detail for this section.*
>
> **Human rights due diligence integration — operational detail.** Our approach to AI in HRDD operates on a simple premise: where AI affects people, the impact is a human rights matter, and we handle it through the same methodology we apply to other human rights matters. We do not maintain a separate AI human rights program; we extend our existing process to address AI-related impacts.
>
> *UNGPs methodology applied to AI.* Our HRDD process follows the cycle established by UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights: identifying actual and potential adverse impacts (Principle 18), prioritizing them based on severity, scope, and likelihood (Principle 24 where prioritization is necessary), integrating findings into our policies and risk management (Principle 19), preventing and mitigating impacts through appropriate action (Principle 19), tracking the effectiveness of our response (Principle 20), and communicating about our due diligence (Principle 21). AI-related impacts are addressed at each stage.
>
> *Three populations.* Our HRDD work covers AI-related impacts on (a) our own workforce, (b) workers in our supply chain (Section 8 operationalizes this dimension), and (c) where applicable to our ambition, end-users and affected communities in our wider value chain — distribution and storage activities, customer use of products incorporating AI, and communities affected by our AI deployments.
>
> *Integration with binding HRDD law.* Where we are subject to binding HRDD law — the Norwegian Transparency Act, CSDDD, German LkSG, French Loi de Vigilance, or similar instruments — our AI-related due diligence is integrated into our reporting and assessment cycles under that law, not added as a separate compliance process. Where we are not subject to such law, we still apply UNGPs methodology voluntarily as the global standard.
>
> *Translation with AI Act.* AI Act risk classification informs but does not replace human rights risk assessment, and vice versa. Section 6 Requirement 6.6 operationalizes this translation; `03_translation_layer.md` documents the logic.

## Requirements

### Requirement 7.1 — AI as a category of human rights risk within existing HRDD

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to addressing AI-related impacts on people as a category of human rights risk within its existing human rights due diligence process, applying the same methodology used for other categories (modern slavery, supply chain labor rights, freedom of association, decent wages, occupational health and safety, environmental rights). The company does not operate a separate parallel AI human rights program.

**Tier:** 3 (framework principle — this is the framework's central argument). Tier 1c where HRDD law requires integrated due diligence (CSDDD Article 7 integration into policies and risk management; Norwegian Transparency Act § 4(a) embedding responsible business conduct into the enterprise's policies).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier. The framework's argument that AI fits inside existing HRDD applies whether or not binding HRDD law mandates it.

**Jurisdiction:** Framework-original; informed by CSDDD Article 7 and Norwegian Transparency Act § 4(a) integration obligations.

**Legal logic:** Framework-original; HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Framework-original:* The argument that AI fits inside existing HRDD methodology is the framework's intellectual core.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 17 (human rights due diligence should include impacts on those whose human rights the enterprise causes, contributes to, or is directly linked to).
- *Binding (where applicable):* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 7 (integration of due diligence into policies and risk management systems).
- *Binding (where applicable):* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4(a) (embed responsible business conduct in the enterprise's policies).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit explicitly to integrated AI HRDD rather than parallel AI compliance, name the company's existing HRDD process or methodology, identify how AI-related risks enter that process, and avoid creating parallel structures (separate AI human rights committee disconnected from the HRDD governance function, separate AI grievance channel disconnected from existing whistleblowing).

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to integrated AI HRDD, names the existing HRDD process or methodology, identifies how AI risks enter that process, and avoids parallel structures.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions integration in general terms but does not name the existing HRDD process or methodology, or describes integration that is inconsistent with the company's actual operational structure.
- *Missing:* The policy treats AI as a parallel compliance track separate from HRDD, or sets up AI-specific human rights structures duplicating existing HRDD infrastructure.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded a Transparency Act report or CSDDD due diligence document, the Generator names that specific process by name and integrates AI as a category within it. (2) If the company has uploaded sustainability or human rights policy material with HRDD methodology references, the Generator draws on that voice. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language committing to integrated AI HRDD with UNGPs methodology as the anchor, jurisdictional tailoring based on the configuration profile (Norwegian, EU, UK, German contexts produce different specific references).*

### Requirement 7.2 — AI-related risk identification across operations and value chain

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to systematically identifying where AI use creates actual and potential adverse impacts on people. Identification covers the company's own workforce, workers in the supply chain (cross-referenced to Section 8), and — where ambition reaches Tier B+ or where binding law applies — end-users, affected communities, and others in the wider value chain. Identification considers AI use in employment decisions, customer-facing processes, supply chain operations, safety-critical contexts, and other deployments where AI may cause or contribute to adverse impacts.

**Tier:** 1c where bound by HRDD law requiring impact identification (CSDDD Article 8, Norwegian Transparency Act § 4); Tier 2 (UNGPs Principle 18 methodology) for companies operating HRDD voluntarily.

**Scope:** Own workforce and supply chain workers activate at Tier A and Tier B ambition. Value chain reach (end-users, affected communities) activates at Tier C ambition; Tier B companies see this as the ambition path.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 8; Norwegian Transparency Act § 4; UK Modern Slavery Act § 54 (informs reporting); UNGPs Principle 18.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 8 (identifying and assessing actual and potential adverse impacts).
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 18 (the enterprise should identify and assess any actual or potential adverse human rights impacts).
- *Interpretive:* OHCHR B-Tech Project on AI and human rights, on identification of AI-related impacts.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 8 of this rubric (supply chain AI risk identification).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to systematic AI-related risk identification, address the three population scopes, name the specific contexts where AI use is assessed (employment, customer-facing, supply chain, safety-critical), and integrate with the company's broader identification methodology.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to systematic identification, addresses applicable populations (gated by ambition), names the contexts where AI is assessed, integrates with existing methodology.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to identification but does not address all applicable populations, or treats identification narrowly (e.g., only own workforce when supply chain reach is required by Transparency Act).
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on systematic identification, or treats AI risk as identified only when surfaced reactively rather than proactively.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded HRDD methodology with impact identification procedures, the Generator extracts the existing approach. (2) If the company has uploaded materiality assessments or sustainability strategy with identified human rights material topics, the Generator integrates AI within those. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language meeting UNGPs Principle 18, CSDDD Article 8, and Transparency Act § 4 minimum, with explicit cross-reference to Section 8 for supply chain dimensions and configuration-gated value chain treatment.*

### Requirement 7.3 — Salience-based prioritization of AI-related risks

**Statement.** Where the company has identified multiple AI-related human rights risks that cannot be addressed simultaneously, the policy commits to prioritization based on salience — the severity of the adverse impact (scale, scope, irremediability), the likelihood of occurrence, and the company's leverage to address the risk. Prioritization is risk-based rather than commercial-criteria-based: a small high-risk AI deployment may receive more attention than a large low-risk one. The company does not deprioritize AI risks because they affect populations the company considers less commercially material.

**Tier:** 1c where CSDDD Article 9 prioritization requirement applies; Tier 2 (UNGPs Principle 24, OECD Due Diligence Guidance) for companies operating HRDD voluntarily.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 9; UNGPs Principle 24; OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 9 (prioritisation of identified actual and potential adverse impacts).
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 24 (where it is necessary to prioritize actions to address actual and potential adverse human rights impacts, business enterprises should first seek to prevent and mitigate those that are most severe).
- *Interpretive:* OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct (2018), particularly on prioritization.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 8 Requirement 8.3 (supply chain AI risk prioritization).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to salience-based prioritization for AI risks, define salience or reference the UNGPs/OECD methodology, exclude commercial criteria as the primary basis for prioritization, and address how prioritization affects allocation of resources to risk response.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to salience-based prioritization, defines salience or references methodology, excludes commercial-only criteria, addresses resource allocation.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to prioritization but does not define salience, or distinguishes only by commercial criteria.
- *Missing:* The policy treats all AI risks identically, or prioritizes based on contract value, supplier size, or other commercial factors alone.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded HRDD methodology with salience-based prioritization, the Generator extracts that approach. (2) If the company has uploaded materiality assessment methodology (CSRD/ESRS), the Generator integrates AI-related salience with materiality logic. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language naming the UNGPs Principle 24 / OECD methodology, with explicit exclusion of commercial-only criteria.*

### Requirement 7.4 — Prevention and mitigation of AI-related adverse impacts

**Statement.** Where AI-related adverse impacts on people are identified, the policy commits the company to taking appropriate action to prevent the impact (where the impact is potential), mitigate the impact (where the impact is actual but ongoing), or end the impact (where the impact can be stopped). The action taken is proportionate to the company's involvement: directly causing the impact, contributing to it, or being directly linked to it through business relationships. The company exercises leverage where it has it, and seeks to build leverage where it does not.

**Tier:** 1c where CSDDD Articles 10–11 and Transparency Act § 4 apply; Tier 2 (UNGPs Principles 19, 22) for voluntary application.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier where adverse impacts are identified.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Articles 10 (preventing potential adverse impacts), 11 (bringing actual adverse impacts to an end); Norwegian Transparency Act § 4; UNGPs Principles 19, 22.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Articles 10, 11.
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principles 19 (integrating findings and taking appropriate action) and 22 (remediation).
- *Interpretive:* OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (2023 update).
- *Cross-reference:* Section 8 Requirement 8.7 (supply chain remediation).
- *Cross-reference:* Section 3 Requirement 3.5 (worker consultation before AI deployment) — prevention and mitigation for own workforce impacts requires consultation with workers and their representatives.

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to prevention, mitigation, or ending of AI-related adverse impacts, address the three involvement categories (causing, contributing to, directly linked to), commit to exercising and building leverage, and specify the kinds of action taken (process changes, deployment halt, supplier engagement, etc.).

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to prevention/mitigation/ending action, addresses the three involvement categories, addresses leverage, specifies action types.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to action but does not distinguish involvement categories, or addresses some action types but not others (e.g., commits to process changes but not to deployment halt where required).
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on action when impacts are identified, or treats identification as the endpoint rather than the starting point of HRDD.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded HRDD methodology with prevention/mitigation procedures, the Generator extracts that approach. (2) If the company has uploaded remediation policies or supplier engagement procedures, the Generator integrates AI dimensions. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language meeting CSDDD Articles 10–11 minimum and UNGPs Principle 19, addressing the involvement categories and leverage concept.*

### Requirement 7.5 — Remediation when AI causes or contributes to adverse impacts

**Statement.** Where AI use has caused or contributed to adverse human rights impacts, the policy commits the company to providing for or cooperating in remediation through legitimate processes. The company provides access to grievance mechanisms (cross-references Section 10) appropriate to the severity of the impact, takes responsibility for impacts the company has caused or contributed to, and engages with affected stakeholders on remediation design.

**Tier:** 1c where binding under CSDDD Article 12 and Transparency Act § 4 remediation obligations; Tier 2 (UNGPs Principles 22, 29) for voluntary application.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier where AI has caused or contributed to adverse impacts.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 12 (remediation of actual adverse impacts); Norwegian Transparency Act § 4; UNGPs Principles 22 (remediation) and 29 (grievance mechanisms).

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 12 (remediation of actual adverse impacts).
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principles 22, 29.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 10 of this rubric (grievance and remedy mechanisms); Section 8 Requirement 8.7 (supply chain remediation).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to remediation where impacts have occurred, distinguish the company's responsibility based on involvement (causing, contributing to, directly linked to), cross-reference Section 10 grievance mechanisms, address engagement with affected stakeholders, and avoid disclaiming responsibility where the company has caused or contributed.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to remediation, distinguishes involvement categories, cross-references grievance mechanisms, addresses stakeholder engagement, avoids responsibility disclaimers.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions remediation but does not distinguish involvement, or references grievance mechanisms without confirming they are accessible to AI-affected stakeholders.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on remediation, or disclaims company responsibility for AI-caused impacts.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded a remediation policy, grievance procedure, or whistleblowing channel description, the Generator extracts the existing remediation framework and ensures AI-affected stakeholders have access. (2) If the company has uploaded a Code of Conduct with remediation commitments, the Generator draws on that voice. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language meeting CSDDD Article 12 and UNGPs Principles 22, 29 minimum, with cross-reference to Section 10.*

### Requirement 7.6 — Tracking effectiveness of AI-related due diligence

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to tracking the effectiveness of its AI-related human rights due diligence — whether identified risks are being prevented, mitigated, ended, or remediated; whether the methodology is producing useful information; whether stakeholder feedback indicates the work is reaching the people affected. Tracking uses qualitative and quantitative indicators appropriate to the impacts being addressed and is integrated with the company's broader tracking of HRDD effectiveness rather than operated as a separate AI-specific monitoring track.

**Tier:** 1c where CSDDD Article 15 monitoring requirement applies; Tier 2 (UNGPs Principle 20) for voluntary application.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 15 (monitoring); Norwegian Transparency Act § 4 (continuous due diligence); UNGPs Principle 20.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 15.
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 20.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 6 Requirement 6.9 (ongoing reassessment); Section 8 Requirement 8.6 (supply chain monitoring).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to tracking effectiveness, integrate with broader HRDD tracking (not separate AI monitoring), use appropriate indicators, address stakeholder feedback as a tracking source, and address cadence (at minimum annual per CSDDD Article 15, with more frequent review where warranted).

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to tracking effectiveness, integrates with broader HRDD tracking, addresses indicators and stakeholder feedback, specifies cadence.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions monitoring but does not integrate with HRDD tracking, or specifies cadence without indicators.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on tracking effectiveness, or treats tracking as a separate AI-specific exercise.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded HRDD monitoring procedures or reporting cycles, the Generator extracts the existing tracking approach. (2) If the company has uploaded sustainability reporting with effectiveness indicators, the Generator draws on those. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language meeting CSDDD Article 15 and UNGPs Principle 20 minimum, with explicit integration into the broader HRDD tracking framework.*

**Statement.** Where the company is subject to binding HRDD reporting obligations (Norwegian Transparency Act § 5 reports, CSDDD Article 16 reporting, UK Modern Slavery Act § 54 statements, CSRD/ESRS sustainability reports), the policy commits to integrating AI-related due diligence findings into those mandatory reports rather than treating them as a separate disclosure track. AI is addressed within the report's existing structure for material topics (e.g., own workforce, value chain workers, affected communities under ESRS S1, S2, S4).

**Tier:** 1b where binding reporting obligations apply. Deactivates as "Not applicable" for companies not subject to such reporting; surfaces as voluntary alignment opportunity per Section 4 Requirement 4.6 voluntary public statement.

**Scope:** Activates conditionally based on Axis 1 jurisdictional exposure to binding HRDD reporting law.

**Jurisdiction:** All HRDD reporting instruments documented in Section 8 Requirement 8.9.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding (where applicable):* All sources cited in Section 8 Requirement 8.9.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 4 Requirement 4.5 (public disclosure); Section 8 Requirement 8.9 (supply chain reporting integration).

**What to look for in a policy.** Where the company is in scope of binding HRDD reporting, the policy should commit to integrating AI findings into mandatory reports, name the specific reports, address material topic integration (ESRS S1/S2/S4 where CSRD applies), and avoid creating a separate AI disclosure track.

**Assessment criteria:**

### Requirement 7.9 — Value chain reach (Tier C ambition)

**Statement.** Where the company's ambition reaches Tier C — own workforce + supply chain + value chain more broadly — the policy commits to addressing AI-related HRDD impacts on end-users, affected communities, and others in the wider value chain. Value chain reach includes downstream activities the company operates or has influence over (distribution, storage, transport of products incorporating AI), customer use of products where the company has reasonable foresight of impact, and communities affected by the company's AI deployments. For companies at Tier A or Tier B ambition, this requirement deactivates substantively but surfaces as the ambition path the company could opt into.

**Tier:** Tier 1c where CSDDD requires downstream chain-of-activities consideration (Article 8 and Article 22 references to downstream business partners for distribution and transport); Tier 2 (UNGPs Principle 17 commentary on directly linked impacts) more broadly; Tier 3 (framework principle) for the full Tier C value chain extension.

**Scope:** Activates substantively at Tier C ambition. Visible as ambition path at Tier A and Tier B.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Articles 8, 22 (downstream chain of activities — distribution, transport, storage); UNGPs Principle 17 (directly linked impacts); CSRD/ESRS S4 (affected communities).

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived; framework-original at the Tier C extension.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding (where in CSDDD scope):* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Articles 8, 22.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 17 commentary.
- *Interpretive:* CSRD/ESRS S4 (affected communities).
- *Cross-reference:* Configuration model `01_rubric_configuration_model.md` Axis 2 ambition scope.

**What to look for in a policy.** For Tier C companies, the policy should commit to value chain reach substantively — naming the downstream activities considered, addressing end-user impacts within reasonable foresight, addressing affected communities. For Tier B companies, the policy should acknowledge the ambition path as available. For Tier A companies, the requirement surfaces only as voluntary alignment opportunity.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered (Tier C):* The policy commits to value chain reach substantively, names downstream activities, addresses end-users and affected communities.
- *Partial (Tier C):* The policy commits to Tier C in general terms but does not specify substantive coverage of value chain elements.
- *Visible as ambition path (Tier A, B):* The policy acknowledges the existence of Tier C requirements as the ambition path, with brief reference to what they would add.
