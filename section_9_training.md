# Section 9: Training and competence

**Status:** Working draft v0.2
**Section position:** Section 9 of 12 in the rubric
**Configuration dependencies:** AI Act Article 4 AI literacy obligations apply to providers and deployers of AI systems. Most requirements activate at all ambition tiers because the legal floor demands it. Training scope scales with company size and AI deployment maturity — what AI literacy training means for a 60-person company differs from what it means for a 2,400-employee company.
**Density:** Medium (6 requirements). Section transposes AI Act Article 4 and operationalizes training expectations across the company's roles.
**Mode:** Prescriptive throughout. Training obligations under AI Act and broader competence expectations are determined by binding law and established practice.
**Executive part weight:** Operational-only (no executive commitment block).

## Section purpose

This section establishes how the company builds and maintains the AI literacy and competence needed to operate its AI deployments responsibly. It is the practical foundation of the rest of the policy — every commitment elsewhere in this document depends on the people executing it having sufficient understanding of AI, practical capability to work with AI, and critical thinking to evaluate AI outputs. A policy that requires human oversight, proportionate risk classification, worker consultation, and integration with HRDD methodology cannot be operationalized by people who do not understand AI well enough to exercise judgment.

The framework treats AI literacy as three connected dimensions:

*Understanding.* Knowing what AI is, what AI systems the company uses, what those systems do and do not do well, what data they use, what their limitations are.

*Practical capability — ways of working with AI.* Knowing how to use AI tools effectively in daily work: how to prompt effectively, how to integrate AI outputs into existing workflows, how to use AI as a starting point rather than a finished product, how to work alongside AI in operational contexts where AI assists machinery, processes, or decisions.

*Critical thinking.* Knowing how to evaluate AI outputs critically — recognizing when outputs are wrong, biased, fabricated, or unreliable; understanding the difference between AI confidence and AI accuracy; recognizing the kinds of errors AI systems characteristically make; exercising judgment to override AI when override is warranted. Critical thinking is the competency that prevents the "AI told me to do it" failure mode, where workers defer to AI outputs without evaluation. Training must develop it actively, not assume it.

The AI Act Article 4 imposes binding AI literacy obligations on providers and deployers of AI systems, requiring measures to ensure to their best extent a sufficient level of AI literacy of staff and other persons dealing with the operation and use of AI systems on their behalf. The framework operationalizes this obligation across all three dimensions above and extends it to address the role-specific competencies needed for the governance body, accountable executives, and workers affected by AI.

The framework's distinctive position in this section is that training is not a one-time compliance activity. It is a continuous capability-building function that scales with AI deployment maturity and evolving regulation. Two cautions inform the section's approach.

The first is that training cannot substitute for governance. A common pattern in AI compliance is to address gaps through training rather than through structural change — "we'll train people to use the AI carefully" instead of "we'll redesign the deployment to address the risk we identified." Training has its place, but it operates alongside governance, not as a replacement for it.

The second is that training cannot substitute for worker consultation. Training informs workers about AI; consultation gives workers a voice in AI deployment decisions affecting them. These are different functions with different purposes. A policy that commits to extensive training but treats consultation as a "communicate the decision" function fails the worker rights commitments in Section 3.

The section is weighted operational-only because training commitments do not warrant executive-level policy statements. They are operational discipline that lives in implementation rather than in the board-level policy adopted as company commitment.

## Generator behavior for this section

This section is prescriptive. Generator behavior follows the cascading priority:

**Priority 1 — Existing training frameworks.** If the company has uploaded a learning and development policy, mandatory training catalog, competence framework, compliance training program, or specific AI literacy materials, the Generator extracts the existing structure and integrates AI training within it. Training cadences, delivery formats, and accountability allocations from existing frameworks are preserved.

**Priority 2 — Adjacent company materials.** If the company has uploaded a Code of Conduct or People / Own Workforce Policy with learning commitments, the Generator draws on that voice and integration.

**Priority 3 — Foundation language with configuration tailoring.** If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language meeting AI Act Article 4 minimum, with role-specific training expectations scaled by company size. For Sections 9 and 11 (operational-only), the Generator produces no Part 1 executive commitment content — both sections appear only in Part 2 of the policy document.

## Requirements

### Requirement 9.1 — General AI literacy across the workforce

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to ensuring a sufficient level of AI literacy across staff and other persons dealing with the operation and use of AI systems on the company's behalf. AI literacy includes three connected dimensions:

(a) **Understanding** — what AI is, what AI systems the company uses, what those systems do and do not do well, what data they use, what their limitations are.

(b) **Practical capability** — how to work with AI tools effectively in daily tasks: how to prompt effectively, how to integrate AI outputs into existing workflows, how to use AI as a starting point rather than a finished product, how to work alongside AI in operational contexts where AI assists machinery, processes, or decisions.

(c) **Critical thinking** — how to evaluate AI outputs critically: recognizing when outputs are wrong, biased, fabricated, or unreliable; understanding the difference between AI confidence and AI accuracy; exercising judgment to override AI when warranted. Critical thinking is developed actively in training, not assumed.

Training is provided at onboarding for new staff and on a periodic basis for existing staff, with content updated as AI deployment evolves and as the company's AI literacy needs mature.

**Tier:** 1b (binding under AI Act Article 4 for providers and deployers).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier wherever the company is a provider or deployer of AI systems within AI Act scope.

**Jurisdiction:** EU AI Act Article 4 (AI literacy).

**Legal logic:** AI Act-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Article 4 — providers and deployers of AI systems shall take measures to ensure, to their best extent, a sufficient level of AI literacy of their staff and other persons dealing with the operation and use of AI systems on their behalf, taking into account their technical knowledge, experience, education and training and the context the AI systems are to be used in.
- *Interpretive:* European Commission and AI Office guidance on AI literacy (most current version); ISO/IEC 42001:2023 on AI management system competence requirements.

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to general AI literacy across the workforce, address all three literacy dimensions (understanding, practical capability, critical thinking), specify delivery (onboarding for new staff, periodic for existing), and address how content is updated as deployment evolves. Critical thinking should be named explicitly rather than assumed. The commitment is proportionate to the company's actual AI deployment — staff in roles untouched by AI receive lighter coverage than staff working with AI directly.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to general AI literacy, addresses all three dimensions (understanding, practical capability, critical thinking) with critical thinking named explicitly, specifies delivery, addresses content updates, scales proportionate to actual workforce exposure to AI.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to AI training but addresses only one or two of the three dimensions (most commonly understanding without practical capability and critical thinking), or treats AI literacy as one-time at deployment rather than ongoing.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on AI literacy, leaving the company unable to discharge AI Act Article 4 obligations.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded a training catalog, competence framework, or learning and development policy, the Generator integrates AI literacy training within the existing structure — using the company's existing training vocabulary, cadences (annual, biennial, onboarding+periodic), and delivery formats. The Generator ensures all three literacy dimensions are addressed even if the existing framework focuses on one. (2) If the company has uploaded an HR / People / Own Workforce Policy or Code of Conduct with learning commitments, the Generator draws on that voice and integration approach. Where multiple materials are uploaded, the Generator tailors based on which contain relevant content. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language meeting AI Act Article 4 minimum with all three literacy dimensions addressed, onboarding-plus-periodic delivery as the default cadence, and content scoped proportionately to the company's reported AI deployment functions.*

### Requirement 9.2 — Role-specific training for the AI governance body and accountable executives

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to providing role-specific training for the members of the AI governance body (per Section 2 Requirement 2.1) and accountable executives (CEO per Section 2 Requirement 2.2, function heads per Section 2 Requirement 2.4). Role-specific training addresses the substantive obligations under this policy and applicable law (AI Act provider and deployer obligations as applicable, GDPR requirements, HRDD obligations under applicable instruments), the governance body's specific responsibilities (risk classification, deployment approval, review cadence), the integration logic across legal regimes (the translation between AI Act, GDPR, and HRDD documented in `03_translation_layer.md` and Section 6 Requirement 6.6), and critical thinking applied to AI deployment decisions — recognizing when AI-driven recommendations should be questioned, when proposed deployments should be challenged, when seemingly compliant uses warrant deeper scrutiny.

**Tier:** 2 (established best practice in corporate governance and AI compliance). Tier 1b where role-specific training is required to discharge specific binding obligations (e.g., Article 4 literacy proportionate to context of use).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** EU AI Act Article 4 (context-of-use literacy proportionate to role); informed by ISO/IEC 42001 on competence; converging best practice in AI governance literature.

**Legal logic:** AI Act-derived primarily.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Article 4.
- *Interpretive:* ISO/IEC 42001:2023 on competence and awareness requirements for AI management systems.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 2 Requirements 2.1, 2.2, 2.4 (governance body, CEO accountability, discipline-specific accountability).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to role-specific training for the governance body and accountable executives, name the substantive areas covered (this policy's obligations, applicable law, governance responsibilities, regime integration, critical thinking applied to deployment decisions), and specify cadence and delivery for these audiences (likely more substantial than general workforce training, more frequent than annual).

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to role-specific training for governance body and executives, names substantive areas including critical thinking applied to decision-making, specifies cadence and delivery proportionate to role responsibilities.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions executive training but does not differentiate from general workforce training, treats governance body training as occasional rather than systematic, or omits critical thinking applied to deployment decisions.
- *Missing:* The policy provides no role-specific training expectations, treating all staff equivalently regardless of role.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded executive training programs, board education materials, or governance committee induction protocols, the Generator integrates AI-specific training within those structures. (2) If the company has uploaded an HR / People / Own Workforce Policy with executive development commitments, or a compliance training program for senior leadership, the Generator draws on that voice and integration. Where multiple materials are uploaded, the Generator tailors based on which contain relevant executive training content. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language committing to role-specific training for the governance body and executives, with substantive areas mapped to Section 2 accountability allocations and critical thinking addressed explicitly.*

### Requirement 9.3 — Function-specific training for staff with operational AI responsibilities

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to providing function-specific training for staff with operational AI responsibilities. Training is calibrated to the substantive work the function performs under this policy and includes the practical capability needed to perform AI-mediated work in that function effectively, alongside critical thinking applied to the function's typical AI interactions:

- **IT and Information Security** — technical risk classification, AI system operation, security implications, recognizing when AI behavior indicates compromise or malfunction.
- **HR** — AI in employment decisions and worker-affecting deployment, evaluating AI vendor claims about HR tools, recognizing AI-driven bias patterns in hiring or performance.
- **Supply Chain and Procurement** — third-party AI assessment, supplier AI risks, evaluating provider claims about training data and bias mitigation, conducting AI-specific due diligence.
- **Legal and Compliance** — regulatory developments, deployer obligations, GDPR Article 22 and AI Act Article 14 application, critical evaluation of AI Act conformity claims.
- **Sustainability or Human Rights** — AI within HRDD methodology, Section 7 integration, identifying AI-related salient human rights risks.
- **Operational functions working alongside AI in safety-critical or process-critical contexts** — practical capability to work with AI-assisted machinery, equipment, or processes; recognizing when AI behavior warrants intervention; understanding when to override and when to follow AI recommendations.

**Tier:** 2 (established best practice). Tier 1b where the function is responsible for discharging specific binding obligations (e.g., Legal/Compliance training on AI Act obligations).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier wherever the company has these functions and AI deployment.

**Jurisdiction:** Same as 9.2.

**Legal logic:** AI Act-derived primarily.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Article 4.
- *Interpretive:* ISO/IEC 42001:2023 on competence requirements.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 2 Requirement 2.4 (discipline-specific accountability allocation); Section 3 Requirement 3.6 (AI in safety-critical worker contexts).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to function-specific training, identify which functions receive it (mapping to Section 2.4 accountability allocation), describe the substantive areas for each function including both procedural knowledge and practical capability to work with AI in the function's typical contexts, address critical thinking specific to the function, and address how function-specific training integrates with general AI literacy (9.1) rather than substituting for it.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to function-specific training, identifies functions, describes substantive areas per function including practical capability and critical thinking, addresses integration with general literacy.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to function-specific training for some functions but not others, focuses on procedural knowledge without practical capability, or does not differentiate function-specific content meaningfully from general literacy.
- *Missing:* The policy provides only general AI literacy without function-specific differentiation, or treats AI training as exclusively IT's concern.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded function-specific training catalogs or compliance training mapping (e.g., GDPR training for HR, anti-corruption training for procurement), the Generator integrates AI-specific function training within the existing structure. (2) If the company has uploaded an HR / People / Own Workforce Policy with function-specific development commitments, or role profiles and competence frameworks naming function expertise areas, the Generator integrates AI dimensions. Where multiple materials are uploaded, the Generator tailors based on which contain relevant content. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language identifying the functions named in Section 2.4 with substantive areas mapped to each function's accountability scope, including practical capability and critical thinking specific to the function's typical AI interactions.*

### Requirement 9.4 — Training on AI Act prohibited practices and worker rights

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to providing training on AI Act Article 5 prohibited practices (cross-references Section 3 Requirement 3.8 and Section 4 Requirement 4.4) and on worker rights regarding AI (cross-references Section 3). Training ensures that staff making AI deployment decisions know which practices the company excludes, understand the worker rights the company commits to respecting, and can recognize situations where AI deployment would conflict with these commitments. Training on prohibited practices is provided at substantive depth to staff making deployment decisions (IT, HR, procurement, governance body); broader awareness is provided across the workforce so staff can raise concerns when they observe potential violations.

**Tier:** 1b (binding under AI Act Article 4 with reference to Article 5 prohibitions; effective discharge of Article 5 obligations requires staff awareness of prohibitions).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** EU AI Act Articles 4, 5.

**Legal logic:** AI Act-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Articles 4, 5.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 3 Requirement 3.8 (prohibited AI uses affecting workers, including AI-driven worker surveillance); Section 4 Requirement 4.4 (emotion recognition and biometric categorization disclosure).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to training on prohibited practices and worker rights, scale depth by audience (deeper for decision-makers, broader awareness across workforce), cross-reference Section 3 and Section 4, and address recognition of potential violations including the framework's position on AI-driven worker surveillance.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to training on prohibited practices and worker rights, scales depth by audience, cross-references the relevant rubric sections, addresses violation recognition.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to AI Act compliance training in general terms but does not specifically address prohibited practices or worker rights, or limits training to staff in specific roles without broader workforce awareness.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on training around prohibited practices or worker rights, leaving the company exposed to inadvertent breaches.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded a Code of Conduct training program, ethics training, or compliance training, the Generator integrates AI-specific content addressing prohibited practices and worker rights. (2) If the company has uploaded an HR / People / Own Workforce Policy with worker-rights training commitments, or worker-rights training materials, the Generator draws on that voice. Where multiple materials are uploaded, the Generator tailors based on which contain relevant content. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language addressing AI Act Article 5 prohibitions specifically (worker surveillance per the framework's position, emotion recognition in workplaces, social scoring, biometric categorization for sensitive characteristics), with audience-scaled depth.*

### Requirement 9.5 — Training cadence and content updates

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to a training cadence appropriate to the pace of AI deployment and regulatory change. Foundational AI literacy training is provided at onboarding plus on a periodic basis (typically annual). Role-specific and function-specific training operates at higher cadence (typically annual or more frequent for legal/compliance and the governance body, with ad-hoc updates as regulation evolves or deployment changes materially). The policy commits to updating training content as the company's AI deployment evolves, as AI Act and related guidance is updated, and as the company's HRDD findings surface new risk categories.

**Tier:** 2 (established best practice). The AI Act's "to the best extent" framing in Article 4 implies an ongoing rather than one-time obligation.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** EU AI Act Article 4 (read with "best extent" implying ongoing obligation); ISO/IEC 42001 on competence maintenance.

**Legal logic:** AI Act-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Article 4.
- *Interpretive:* ISO/IEC 42001:2023 on competence and continual improvement.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 2 Requirement 2.7 (governance review cycle — training updates align with the broader policy review cycle).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to training cadence appropriate to AI deployment pace and regulatory change, specify cadences for different audiences (workforce, function-specific, executive/governance body), address content updates as deployment and regulation evolve, and integrate training review with the broader policy review cycle in Section 2.7.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to training cadence, specifies cadences per audience, addresses content updates, integrates with broader policy review cycle.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions training cadence in general terms but does not differentiate by audience or address content updates, or treats training as static.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on cadence and updates, treating training as one-time at policy adoption.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded a training cadence schedule, learning calendar, or compliance training cycle, the Generator integrates AI training within the existing cadence framework. (2) If the company has uploaded an HR / People / Own Workforce Policy with development cycles or the broader governance review cycle materials, the Generator aligns training updates with policy review timing. Where multiple materials are uploaded, the Generator tailors based on which contain relevant content. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language with at-minimum-annual cadence for foundational literacy, more frequent cadence for role-specific training, and update commitment triggered by regulatory change, deployment change, or HRDD findings.*

### Requirement 9.6 — Training accountability and integration with existing learning frameworks

**Statement.** The policy assigns accountability for AI training to a designated function (typically HR / People Operations in larger companies, or the AI governance body lead in smaller companies — per Section 2 Requirement 2.4 discipline allocation). Training is integrated with the company's existing learning and development infrastructure rather than operated as a separate AI-specific training track. Where the company uses a learning management system, AI training is delivered through it; where the company has an existing mandatory training catalog, AI training is added to it; where the company conducts competence assessment, AI competence is included.

**Tier:** 2 (established best practice).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** Framework-original structure, informed by AI Act Article 4 implementation expectations and ISO/IEC 42001 management system integration.

**Legal logic:** AI Act-derived primarily.

**Source citations:**
- *Interpretive:* ISO/IEC 42001:2023 on integration with existing management systems.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 2 Requirement 2.4 (discipline-specific accountability — HR or People function accountable for worker-affecting commitments including training); Section 1 Requirement 1.4 (continuity principle — integration with existing infrastructure).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should identify the function accountable for AI training, integrate AI training with existing learning frameworks, address delivery mechanisms (LMS, training catalog, competence assessment), and avoid creating a parallel AI-specific training track disconnected from the company's broader learning infrastructure.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy identifies the accountable function, integrates AI training with existing learning frameworks, addresses delivery mechanisms, avoids parallel structures.
- *Partial:* The policy assigns AI training to a function but does not integrate with existing learning frameworks, or treats AI training as separate from existing training infrastructure without justification.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on training accountability, or creates an AI-specific training track that duplicates or conflicts with existing learning infrastructure.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded learning and development structure, LMS information, or HR / People / Own Workforce Policy with training accountability assignments, the Generator integrates AI training accountability within the existing structure. (2) If the company has uploaded competence frameworks, the Generator references those for AI competence integration. Where multiple materials are uploaded, the Generator tailors based on which contain relevant content. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language assigning training accountability per Section 2.4 (typically HR), committing to integration with existing learning infrastructure where it exists.*

## How this section activates across company profiles

**Larger company with mature learning infrastructure (e.g., 2,000+ employees, formal LMS, mandatory training catalog, defined competence frameworks):** All six requirements activate. Integration with existing learning infrastructure (9.6) produces substantial reuse of existing systems. Role-specific (9.2) and function-specific (9.3) training is detailed and proportionate to organizational complexity. Training cadence (9.5) operates within established review cycles.

**Mid-size company with moderate learning infrastructure (e.g., 250–1,000 employees, some formalized training, mix of online and in-person delivery):** All six requirements activate. Existing learning infrastructure is leaner; the framework's foundation language fills more gaps. Function-specific training (9.3) may be lighter where the company has fewer specialized functions.

**Small company with minimal learning infrastructure (e.g., 50–250 employees, informal training, no LMS):** All six requirements activate, but operationally lighter. General AI literacy (9.1) and prohibited practices/worker rights training (9.4) are universal. Role-specific (9.2) operates with the governance body as a smaller group. Function-specific (9.3) is proportionate to the company's actual functions. Integration with existing learning frameworks (9.6) may mean integration with employee handbook updates and team meetings rather than formal LMS delivery. The framework's commitment is to proportionate training, not to formal compliance training infrastructure that may be disproportionate for a small company.

This activation pattern reinforces a key framework property: training obligations under AI Act Article 4 apply universally, but what they look like operationally scales by company maturity. A small company is not exempt from AI literacy obligations; the framework expects them to operate proportionate training, integrated with their existing (lighter) learning infrastructure.

## Open items for review

**Length and density.** Section 9 came in at 6 requirements at medium density, operational-only weighting. Worth your view on whether 6 requirements is the right count or whether some could merge (e.g., 9.2 governance body training and 9.3 function-specific training could merge as a single "role-specific training" requirement with sub-elements; 9.5 cadence and 9.6 integration could merge).

**AI Act Article 4 interpretation.** The "to the best extent" framing in Article 4 leaves room for proportionality interpretation. Some companies will interpret this conservatively (extensive training for everyone); others will interpret it minimally (basic awareness only). The framework's requirements lean proportionate-but-substantive — not minimal compliance but not over-engineered either. Worth your view on whether the balance is right.

**Worker consultation vs training distinction (Section 9 vs Section 3).** Section 9 is about training (informing workers about AI). Section 3 Requirement 3.5 is about consultation (giving workers voice in deployment decisions). The section narrative addresses this distinction explicitly. Worth your view on whether the distinction is sufficiently clear, especially since training and consultation are often conflated in corporate practice.

**No executive commitment block.** Per the configuration model v0.4 section weighting, Section 9 is operational-only and produces no Part 1 content. This was a deliberate decision because training commitments do not warrant executive-level policy statements. Worth confirming this is right — an argument exists for a brief executive line ("We commit to AI literacy across our workforce") even at operational-only weighting, similar to a single sentence reference. My recommendation is to keep the section operational-only as agreed, but flag for your view.
