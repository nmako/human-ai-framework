# Section 10: Grievance and remedy mechanisms

**Status:** Working draft v0.1
**Section position:** Section 10 of 12 in the rubric
**Configuration dependencies:** Most requirements activate at all ambition tiers because legal floor (UNGPs Principle 29 / 31 anchoring, CSDDD Article 14 complaints procedures for in-scope companies, EU Whistleblower Directive 2019/1937 for internal whistleblowing) applies regardless of ambition. Reach across affected populations (own workers / supplier workers / customers / affected communities) scales by ambition tier. Some requirements are hybrid mode where company-specific channel structure determines the content; others are prescriptive where the framework takes universal positions.
**Density:** Medium-heavy (7 requirements). Section operationalizes the framework's accountability commitments by making them accessible to affected people through grievance channels.
**Mode:** Mixed. Hybrid for requirements where company channel structure determines content (10.1, 10.2); prescriptive for requirements where the framework takes universal positions (10.3 through 10.7).
**Executive part weight:** Medium (40–60 words target).

---

## Section purpose

This section establishes how affected people raise complaints about AI-related harms and how the company responds. It is where the framework's accountability commitments become operationally accessible to the people the policy is meant to protect. A policy that commits to worker rights, transparency, non-discrimination, and human rights due diligence but provides no mechanism for affected people to raise concerns when those commitments are not met has not made the commitments operational.

The framework's position on grievance mechanisms is that they extend the company's existing grievance infrastructure rather than building parallel AI-specific channels. The continuity principle from Section 1 Requirement 1.4 applies: a company that already operates a whistleblowing channel under the EU Whistleblower Directive, a customer complaints procedure, a supplier code hotline, and (where applicable) a CSDDD Article 14 complaints procedure has the infrastructure to address AI-related grievances. What is needed is to ensure that infrastructure is accessible to people raising AI-related concerns, that AI-related grievances are recognized and routed appropriately, and that the effectiveness criteria established by UNGPs Principle 31 apply to AI grievances as they apply to other grievances.

Several binding instruments and frameworks converge in this section. UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights Principle 29 establishes that business enterprises should provide for or cooperate in the remediation of adverse human rights impacts they have caused or contributed to, including through operational-level grievance mechanisms. UNGPs Principle 31 establishes effectiveness criteria for non-judicial grievance mechanisms: legitimacy, accessibility, predictability, equitability, transparency, rights-compatibility, source of continuous learning, and (for operational-level mechanisms) engagement and dialogue. CSDDD Article 14 requires in-scope companies to establish a notification mechanism and complaints procedure accessible to natural and legal persons that may be affected by adverse impacts. The EU Whistleblower Directive (2019/1937) requires internal whistleblowing channels for most companies above certain thresholds. The Norwegian Transparency Act § 6 provides a right to information that supports grievance work. Sector-specific instruments (Modern Slavery Acts, GDPR Article 77 supervisory authority complaints, Equality Act complaints) provide additional pathways for AI-affected populations.

The framework's distinctive position is that AI-related grievances are human rights grievances and operate through human rights grievance infrastructure. The framework does not require companies to build a separate "AI ethics complaints" channel disconnected from existing remediation pathways. It requires that existing pathways accept and effectively handle AI-related concerns, and that they are accessible to all affected populations — including supplier workers and affected communities who often have no direct relationship with the company.

The section is weighted medium executive treatment because grievance commitments matter at policy level (affected people need to know the company commits to providing pathways) but the operational substance — channel names, contact details, routing logic — is appropriately operational rather than board-level.

>
> *UNGPs Principle 31 effectiveness criteria.* Our mechanisms meet the effectiveness criteria for non-judicial grievance mechanisms: legitimacy (trust of those who may use them), accessibility (known to and reachable by potential users without barriers), predictability (clear procedure and timeframes), equitability (information and support to engage on fair, informed terms), transparency (parties informed about progress), rights-compatibility (outcomes consistent with international human rights), source of continuous learning (lessons drawn from experience), and engagement and dialogue (consultation with intended user groups).

>
> *Tracking and learning.* Patterns in AI-related grievances inform our AI risk assessment, our governance review, and our training updates. A grievance that surfaces a systemic issue is treated as such, not as an isolated case.

## Requirements

### Requirement 10.1 — Existing grievance channels accessible for AI-related concerns (hybrid mode)

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to ensuring AI-related concerns are within scope of the company's existing grievance channels. The policy identifies the specific channels available, names them, provides accessible contact information for each, and confirms that AI-related concerns are explicitly within each channel's scope. The policy does not require affected people to identify a complaint as "AI-related" to access the channel — concerns are accepted in the language affected people use to describe them.

**Tier:** 1b where bound by EU Whistleblower Directive 2019/1937 (internal channel obligation), CSDDD Article 14 (complaints procedure for in-scope companies), or sector-specific complaints obligations. Tier 2 (UNGPs Principle 29 anchored) for channels going beyond binding minimum.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier. Channel reach scales by population coverage (own workers; supplier workers and value chain; customers and affected communities) per Axis 2.

**Jurisdiction:** EU Whistleblower Directive (Directive (EU) 2019/1937) — internal whistleblowing channel obligation for companies above thresholds; EU CSDDD Article 14 — notification mechanism and complaints procedure for in-scope companies; Norwegian Transparency Act § 6 — right to information supporting grievance; UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights Principle 29; sector-specific complaints regimes (Modern Slavery Acts, sectoral ombudsman regimes); GDPR Article 77 (right to lodge complaint with supervisory authority) as a parallel external pathway.

**Legal logic:** Cross-cutting — combines whistleblowing law, HRDD law, and HRDD methodology.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2019/1937 on the protection of persons who report breaches of Union law (Whistleblower Directive).
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 14 (notification mechanism and complaints procedure).
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 6.
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR), Article 77 (right to lodge complaint with supervisory authority).
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 29.

**Company input.** *Please tell us about your existing grievance and complaints channels. Specifically, we'd like to know: (a) the names of your existing channels (whistleblowing, customer complaints, supplier hotline, employee grievance procedure, etc.); (b) the contact details for each (email, phone, URL); (c) the function or role accountable for each channel; (d) any specific scope statements (e.g., what each channel does and does not cover). We will integrate this information directly into your policy.*

[FIELD: Name of internal whistleblowing or employee grievance channel]

[FIELD: Contact details for the internal channel (email, phone, URL, in-person path)]

[FIELD: Function accountable for the internal channel]

[FIELD: Customer complaints channel name and contact details]

[FIELD: Supplier-facing complaints channel name and contact details]

[FIELD: Any other grievance channels in operation (specify scope)]

**Foundation language (used by the Generator if no company input is provided, or as the basis for enrichment when company input is provided):** *AI-related concerns are within scope of our existing grievance channels: our internal [whistleblowing / employee grievance] channel for our workers; our [customer complaints procedure] for customers; our [supplier hotline or external channel] for workers in our value chain. Each channel accepts AI-related concerns explicitly. We do not require affected people to identify their complaint as "AI-related" to access the channel.*

**Assessment criteria:**

- *Strong:* The company's submitted channel information names specific channels with contact details, addresses all relevant populations (own workers, supplier workers, customers, affected communities), confirms AI-related concerns are within scope, names the accountable function for each channel, and confirms accessibility without requiring affected people to characterize complaints as AI-related.
- *Can be strengthened:* The company's information names channels but omits contact details, omits one or more population groups, or limits AI scope to one channel when multiple channels should accept AI-related concerns. Specific suggested strengthenings: add the missing population's pathway; extend AI scope to all relevant channels; provide complete contact details.
- *Conflicts with human rights or binding law:* The company's submitted information restricts grievance access in ways incompatible with binding obligations — for example, "supplier workers must raise concerns through their own employer only" (conflicts with CSDDD Article 14 obligation to make complaints procedure accessible to "natural and legal persons that may be affected by adverse impacts"), or "AI-related concerns are excluded from our whistleblowing channel because they are operational not ethical" (conflicts with the UNGPs Principle 31 accessibility criterion). These framings cannot pass through.

**Assessment criteria (when no company channel information is submitted):** *Covered:* foundation language is included with placeholder phrasing the company can refine, indicating clearly which channel information needs to be populated. *Missing:* the policy is silent on grievance channels for AI-related concerns.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company submitted channel information through the input fields, the Generator integrates the named channels, contact details, and accountable functions directly into the policy output. The Generator produces language that reads as the company's actual operational reality. (2) If the company has uploaded a Whistleblowing policy, Code of Conduct, Supplier Code of Conduct, customer service procedure, or CSDDD Article 14 complaints procedure, the Generator extracts channel names and contact details from those documents. (3) If neither input fields nor uploads provide channel information, the Generator produces foundation language with explicit placeholders ("our internal whistleblowing channel [name]"; "our customer complaints procedure [contact details]") that signal what information needs to be populated. The policy is usable from first generation but visibly incomplete on channel specifics.*

### Requirement 10.2 — Accessibility to affected populations beyond own workers (hybrid mode)

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to ensuring its grievance channels are accessible to affected people beyond own workers — including workers in the company's supply chain, customers and end-users, and affected communities. Accessibility includes practical reachability (channels available in relevant languages, accessible to people without internet, accessible to people without formal organizational relationships with the company), procedural accessibility (clear instructions, support to engage on fair and informed terms), and freedom from barriers (no requirement for legal representation, no fees, no prerequisite of identifying as an "AI" grievance).

**Tier:** 1b where bound by CSDDD Article 14 (which explicitly requires accessibility to natural and legal persons affected by adverse impacts, including their representatives — extending beyond own workers). Tier 2 (UNGPs Principle 31 accessibility criterion) for accessibility going beyond binding minimum.

**Scope:** Activates regardless of ambition tier for own workers and customers; supplier worker and community accessibility activates more strongly at Tier B (supply chain) and Tier C (value chain) ambition.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 14; UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights Principle 31 effectiveness criteria (especially accessibility); OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises Chapter II commentary on grievance accessibility.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 14.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 31.
- *Interpretive:* OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (2023 update).
- *Cross-reference:* Section 7 of this rubric (HRDD integration); Section 8 (supply chain).

**Company input.** *Please tell us how affected people beyond your own workers access your grievance channels. Specifically: (a) how do workers in your supply chain raise concerns about AI use affecting them? (b) how do customers raise AI-related concerns? (c) how do affected communities or third parties raise concerns? If you do not currently have established pathways for some of these populations, please indicate that — the framework will produce language committing to development of those pathways.*

[FIELD: How supplier workers and others in your value chain access grievance channels]

[FIELD: How customers and end-users access AI-related grievance pathways]

[FIELD: How affected communities or third parties access grievance pathways (or whether such pathway is yet to be established)]

[FIELD: Languages supported across your grievance channels]

**Foundation language:** *Our grievance channels are accessible to affected people beyond our own workforce. Workers in our supply chain can raise AI-related concerns through [supplier-facing channel or external mechanism]. Customers raise concerns through [customer pathway]. Affected communities and third parties can raise concerns through [community channel or general pathway]. Channels are available in [languages supported] and do not require legal representation, fees, or AI-specific framing of the complaint to be accessible.*

**Assessment criteria:**

- *Strong:* The company's submitted accessibility approach addresses all affected populations, names specific pathways for each, addresses language and practical accessibility, and where existing pathways are absent, commits to development with specified timeframe.
- *Can be strengthened:* The company addresses some populations but not others, or names pathways without addressing language or practical accessibility, or commits to development of absent pathways without specifying timeframe.
- *Conflicts with human rights or binding law:* The company's approach excludes affected populations explicitly (e.g., "this channel is only for direct employees") in ways incompatible with CSDDD Article 14 obligations or UNGPs Principle 31 accessibility criterion. Cannot pass through.

**Assessment criteria (when no company information is submitted):** *Covered:* foundation language is included with placeholders that make absent pathways visible. *Missing:* the policy is silent on accessibility beyond own workers.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company submitted accessibility information, the Generator integrates it directly. (2) If the company has uploaded Supplier Code of Conduct, customer service materials, or HRDD methodology documenting accessibility approaches, the Generator extracts those. (3) If no information is available and the company is at Tier B or Tier C ambition, the Generator produces foundation language with placeholders for each population and explicit acknowledgment that pathways for absent populations are committed to development.*

### Requirement 10.3 — UNGPs Principle 31 effectiveness criteria for AI grievances

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to ensuring its grievance mechanisms meet the UNGPs Principle 31 effectiveness criteria for non-judicial grievance mechanisms: legitimacy (enabling trust from intended user groups and accountability for the fair conduct of grievance processes), accessibility (known to all intended user groups and providing adequate assistance to overcome access barriers), predictability (clear and known procedure with indicative timeframes and process types), equitability (ensuring aggrieved parties have reasonable access to information, advice, and expertise to engage on fair, informed, and respectful terms), transparency (keeping parties informed about progress and providing sufficient information about the mechanism's performance to build confidence), rights-compatibility (ensuring outcomes and remedies accord with internationally recognized human rights), source of continuous learning (drawing on relevant measures to identify lessons), and engagement and dialogue (consulting intended user groups about design and performance, and focusing on dialogue rather than imposed outcomes).

**Tier:** 2 (UNGPs Principle 31 is established best practice; Tier 1c where binding HRDD law incorporates UNGPs effectiveness criteria implicitly — CSDDD Recital 75 and Article 14 reflect UNGPs methodology).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 31; reflected in CSDDD Article 14 and Recital 75; OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises commentary.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (2011), Principle 31.
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 14 and Recital 75 (incorporating UNGPs methodology by reference).
- *Interpretive:* OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (2023 update).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should explicitly commit to the UNGPs Principle 31 effectiveness criteria, name them or reference them substantively, and connect them to the company's specific grievance practice. The commitment is not abstract — each criterion should map to operational practice.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to the UNGPs Principle 31 criteria, names them or references them substantively, and connects them to specific grievance practice.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions some criteria but not all, or commits to "effective grievance mechanisms" without referencing the UNGPs criteria specifically.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on effectiveness criteria, or commits to grievance handling without quality standards.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded grievance procedure documents or HRDD methodology referencing UNGPs Principle 31, the Generator extracts existing language and integrates. (2) If the company has uploaded a Whistleblowing policy with effectiveness elements (clear procedure, timeframes, anti-retaliation), the Generator draws on that voice and maps to the full UNGPs criteria. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language naming the eight criteria with brief operational implications for each.*

### Requirement 10.4 — Anti-retaliation protection for AI-related grievances

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to protecting any person raising an AI-related grievance in good faith from retaliation. Protection extends to workers, contractors, supplier workers, customers, and any other person using the grievance channels. Retaliation includes adverse employment action, termination of business relationships, harassment, withdrawal of services, and any other adverse treatment connected to the grievance. The policy connects to the EU Whistleblower Directive 2019/1937 protections where applicable and extends those protections to other grievance pathways consistent with the framework's people-first principle.

**Tier:** 1a where binding under the EU Whistleblower Directive (Directive 2019/1937 Article 19 anti-retaliation prohibitions are mandatory for in-scope reports through internal/external channels). Tier 1b where binding under CSDDD Article 14 and sector-specific anti-retaliation provisions. Tier 2 (framework's extension to all grievance pathways) for protection beyond binding minimum.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** EU Whistleblower Directive (Directive (EU) 2019/1937), Articles 19, 21; EU CSDDD Article 14; Norwegian Whistleblowing Act provisions (Arbeidsmiljøloven Chapter 2 A); sector-specific anti-retaliation provisions.

**Legal logic:** Cross-cutting — combines whistleblowing law and HRDD law.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2019/1937, Articles 19 (prohibition of retaliation) and 21 (measures of protection).
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 14 (which references the EU Whistleblower Directive for natural persons making complaints through the complaints procedure).
- *Binding:* Lov om arbeidsmiljø, arbeidstid og stillingsvern mv. (Arbeidsmiljøloven, Norway), Chapter 2 A (whistleblowing in employment relationships).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit explicitly to anti-retaliation protection, extend protection to all categories of people using grievance channels (not just employees), name the kinds of retaliation prohibited, reference applicable binding instruments (Whistleblower Directive, national whistleblowing law), and address how retaliation is itself a separate disciplinary matter when it occurs.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits explicitly to anti-retaliation, extends protection beyond employees, names prohibited categories of retaliation, references binding instruments, addresses retaliation as a separate disciplinary matter.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to anti-retaliation but limits protection to employees only, or addresses retaliation in general terms without naming specific prohibited categories.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on retaliation protection, or treats retaliation as a procedural matter rather than a substantive prohibition.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded a Whistleblowing policy with explicit anti-retaliation provisions, the Generator extracts that language. (2) If the company has uploaded a Code of Conduct addressing retaliation, the Generator draws on that voice. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language meeting Whistleblower Directive Article 19, CSDDD Article 14, and applicable national whistleblowing law minimum, with explicit extension to all grievance channel users.*

### Requirement 10.5 — Routing of AI-related grievances to substantive response

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to routing AI-related grievances received through any channel to the function or governance body responsible for substantive response. The AI governance body (Section 2 Requirement 2.1) receives grievances raising AI-specific issues. Worker-affecting AI grievances route to HR with AI governance body coordination; supplier-related AI grievances route to procurement with HRDD function coordination; customer-affecting AI grievances route to the responsible business function with governance body notification where systemic issues are surfaced. The routing logic ensures grievances reach decision-makers with authority to investigate and respond.

**Tier:** 2 (established best practice). Tier 1b where binding under CSDDD Article 14 (which requires the complaints procedure to result in proper handling of complaints).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 14; UNGPs Principle 31 (predictability criterion); EU Whistleblower Directive 2019/1937 Article 11 (follow-up to internal reports).

**Legal logic:** Cross-cutting.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 14.
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2019/1937, Article 11.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 31.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 2 Requirement 2.1 (AI governance body); Section 2 Requirement 2.4 (functional accountability).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to systematic routing of AI-related grievances, identify how grievances reach the AI governance body when AI-specific issues are surfaced, name the routing logic for different grievance categories (worker-affecting, supplier-related, customer-affecting), and ensure grievances reach decision-makers with substantive authority.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to systematic routing, identifies governance body involvement for AI-specific issues, names routing logic by grievance category, ensures substantive decision-maker reach.
- *Partial:* The policy addresses some routing dimensions but not others, or commits to routing without naming the AI governance body's role.
- *Missing:* The policy treats AI grievances as routed to general grievance handling without AI-specific escalation, or is silent on routing.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded grievance procedure documents naming routing logic, the Generator extracts and integrates AI-specific routing. (2) If the company has uploaded Whistleblowing or HR procedures with escalation paths, the Generator draws on those. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language with routing logic by grievance category, AI governance body involvement for AI-specific issues, and integration with the discipline allocation in Section 2 Requirement 2.4.*

### Requirement 10.6 — Integration of grievances with HRDD remediation

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to integrating grievances that surface adverse human rights impacts into its HRDD remediation work (Section 7 Requirement 7.5). Where a grievance surfaces an adverse impact the company has caused, contributed to, or is directly linked to, the response includes substantive remediation consistent with UNGPs Principles 22 and 29 and (where applicable) CSDDD Article 12. Grievance handling does not substitute for remediation; it identifies where remediation is needed and triggers the remediation pathway.

**Tier:** 1c where bound by CSDDD Article 12 (remediation), Norwegian Transparency Act § 4 (cease and remedy obligations), and other HRDD law remediation provisions; Tier 2 (UNGPs methodology) for voluntary application.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier where adverse impacts surface through grievances.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 12; Norwegian Transparency Act § 4; UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights Principles 22, 29.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 12.
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principles 22, 29.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 7 Requirement 7.5 (remediation when AI causes or contributes to adverse impacts); Section 8 Requirement 8.7 (supply chain remediation).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should connect grievance handling to substantive remediation, cross-reference Section 7 Requirement 7.5, distinguish grievance handling (the process of receiving and responding to complaints) from remediation (the substantive provision of remedy), and ensure that grievances surfacing adverse impacts trigger the remediation pathway.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to integration of grievances with remediation, cross-references Section 7, distinguishes grievance handling from remediation, ensures remediation is triggered where adverse impacts surface.
- *Partial:* The policy connects grievances to remediation in general terms but does not cross-reference Section 7 or distinguish the two functions clearly.
- *Missing:* The policy treats grievance handling as separate from remediation, treats grievance response as the endpoint rather than the trigger for remediation, or is silent on the connection.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded remediation procedures or HRDD methodology connecting grievances to remediation, the Generator extracts and integrates. (2) If the company has uploaded a Code of Conduct addressing remediation, the Generator draws on that voice. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language meeting CSDDD Article 12 and UNGPs Principles 22, 29 minimum, with explicit cross-reference to Section 7 Requirement 7.5.*

### Requirement 10.7 — Tracking grievances and learning from patterns

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to tracking grievances received, the substantive issues raised, the response provided, and the outcomes. Tracking enables the company to identify patterns — recurring AI-related issues, systemic concerns surfacing across multiple grievances, gaps in existing controls. Patterns feed into AI risk assessment (Section 6), HRDD work (Section 7), training updates (Section 9), and governance review (Section 2 Requirement 2.7). The tracking system protects confidentiality of individual grievances while enabling aggregate learning.

**Tier:** 2 (established best practice; UNGPs Principle 31 source of continuous learning criterion).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights Principle 31; EU CSDDD Article 14 (implicitly through proper handling obligation); EU Whistleblower Directive 2019/1937 (record-keeping requirements under Article 18).

**Legal logic:** Cross-cutting.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2019/1937, Article 18 (record-keeping of reports).
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 14.
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, Principle 31.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 6 (risk classification); Section 7 (HRDD integration); Section 9 (training); Section 2 Requirement 2.7 (governance review cycle).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to systematic tracking of grievances, name the substantive areas covered (issues, responses, outcomes), address pattern identification, name the connections to AI risk assessment / HRDD / training / governance review, and address confidentiality protection in aggregate reporting.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to systematic tracking, names substantive coverage, addresses pattern identification, names connections to other sections, addresses confidentiality.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to tracking but does not address pattern identification, omits connections to other sections, or treats tracking as administrative rather than learning-focused.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on tracking or treats grievances as discrete events without aggregate learning.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company has uploaded a Whistleblowing policy with record-keeping provisions or grievance tracking methodology, the Generator extracts and integrates AI dimensions. (2) If the company has uploaded HRDD methodology with monitoring elements, the Generator draws on that. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces foundation language meeting EU Whistleblower Directive Article 18 record-keeping minimum, UNGPs Principle 31 continuous learning criterion, and integration with the named other sections.*

## How this section activates across company profiles

**Company with established grievance infrastructure (whistleblowing channel under EU Whistleblower Directive, customer complaints procedure, supplier hotline, formal Code of Conduct):** All seven requirements activate. Hybrid mode requirements (10.1, 10.2) draw rich company-specific channel information from the input fields and from uploaded materials — the Generator output names actual channels by name with actual contact details. Prescriptive requirements (10.3 through 10.7) integrate with the company's existing grievance infrastructure rather than creating parallel structures.

**Company with moderate grievance infrastructure (Whistleblower Directive channel in place, customer complaints procedure exists, supplier-facing pathway less developed):** All seven requirements activate. Hybrid mode requirements name existing channels and flag where pathways for supplier workers or affected communities need development. The framework's commitment is that the policy commits to development rather than excludes the population — language reads "we are establishing a supplier-facing pathway [target date]" rather than "this channel is not available to suppliers."

**Company with minimal grievance infrastructure (small company below Whistleblower Directive thresholds, no formal channel structure):** All seven requirements activate, but operationally lighter. Hybrid mode requirements may show foundation language with prominent placeholders that flag the absence of formal channels and commit the company to establishing channels as it grows. The framework's position is that grievance mechanisms are a baseline expectation for any company — even a 60-employee company can establish a designated email address, a routing accountable role, and the UNGPs Principle 31 effectiveness commitment.

**Company under CSDDD scope:** Requirement 10.1 activates at Tier 1b — the complaints procedure obligation is binding. Requirement 10.2 activates at Tier 1b for the accessibility-to-affected-persons obligation. Requirement 10.4 activates at Tier 1a in connection with EU Whistleblower Directive anti-retaliation. Requirement 10.6 activates at Tier 1c through CSDDD Article 12 remediation.

This activation pattern reinforces a framework property: grievance mechanism expectations are universal at baseline, scale up substantively where binding HRDD law applies, and depend on company-specific structure for operational specifics.

## Open items for review

**Hybrid mode allocation (10.1, 10.2 hybrid; 10.3 through 10.7 prescriptive).** I placed channel-naming and accessibility requirements in hybrid mode because they depend on company-specific structure. Effectiveness criteria, anti-retaliation, routing, integration with remediation, and tracking are prescriptive because they are framework positions or transpositions of binding law. Worth your view on whether this allocation is right or whether routing (10.5) should also be hybrid given that routing depends on the company's specific function structure.

**Section length.** Section 10 came in at 7 requirements at medium-heavy density. The executive commitment (55 words) is within the medium target. The operational substance is significant because grievance is operationally complex — multiple populations, multiple channels, integration with HRDD and other sections. Worth your view on whether 7 requirements is right or whether some could merge (10.5 routing and 10.6 integration could potentially merge as "substantive response to grievances").

**Input field count.** Requirement 10.1 has six input fields. Requirement 10.2 has four. This is more input than any single requirement in earlier sections. The questions are specific and operationally necessary — companies need to populate this information for the policy to be usable. Worth your view on whether the field count is right or whether some fields should be consolidated (e.g., one field for "describe your existing grievance channels" instead of six discrete fields).

**Anti-retaliation tier (10.4 partly Tier 1a).** The EU Whistleblower Directive Article 19 anti-retaliation prohibition is binding for in-scope reports. I tagged 10.4 as Tier 1a where Whistleblower Directive applies and Tier 1b otherwise. This dual tagging is unusual. Alternative: keep 10.4 as Tier 1b throughout and rely on the source citations to make the binding nature visible. Worth your view.

**Tracking confidentiality (10.7).** The requirement balances aggregate learning against individual confidentiality. This balance is operationally tricky — small companies with few grievances may struggle to anonymize patterns. The requirement currently addresses this in general terms. Worth your view on whether more specific language is needed.
