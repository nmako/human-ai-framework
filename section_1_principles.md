## Section purpose

This section sets the principles that govern the company's use of AI in relation to people. It is the foundation that every operational commitment elsewhere in the policy rests on. The principles are not aspirational language; they are commitments the company holds itself accountable to.

These principles are framework-original — they are not transposed from binding law or from any single authoritative framework. They are the position the framework takes on what a Human-AI policy is *for*. Companies that adopt the framework adopt these principles or articulate substantively equivalent ones in their own voice. The Diagnostic evaluates company-submitted language for substantive equivalence, not verbatim language.

This section is one of two in the rubric (along with Section 2: Governance and accountability) where the company's voice should be most visible. Other sections of this policy are largely technical — transpositions of binding obligations under the AI Act, GDPR, CSDDD, the Norwegian Transparency Act, and similar instruments. In those sections the framework prescribes substance because the substance is determined by law. In this section, the framework establishes a foundation and invites the company to express it in their own voice.

It extends our Code of Conduct, our human rights commitments, our commitments to our own workforce, and our existing approach to responsible business — to AI as a new dimension of work, not a separate domain.

## Requirements

### Requirement 1.1 — People first principle: AI assists, does not replace, human responsibility

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to using AI as a tool that supports human work and human judgment, not as a substitute for human responsibility toward affected people. Where AI deployment trades off against worker interests, supplier worker interests, customer interests, or community interests, the policy commits to weighing those interests substantively and not subordinating them to efficiency or cost rationales alone.

**Tier:** 3 (framework principle).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier or jurisdiction.

**Jurisdiction:** Framework-original. Aligned with UNGPs Foundational Principle 11 and OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises Chapter II.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived in spirit; not tied to specific binding obligation.

**Source citations:**
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (2011), Foundational Principles 11–15.
- *Interpretive:* OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (2023 update), Chapter II.
- *Framework-original:* The "people first" framing is a project-original commitment.

**Company input.** *If you have already discussed AI principles internally, or if you have strong preferences on how to frame your commitment to people in AI use, please share them here. The framework will measure your language against the best-practice principles already in our system and produce specific feedback — what is strong in your formulation, what could be strengthened, and where (rare) any framing would conflict with human rights principles or binding law.*

[FIELD: Your principle on the relationship between AI and human responsibility, in your own words]

**Foundation language (used by the Generator if no company input is provided, or as the basis for enrichment when company input is provided):** *People first. AI assists human work; it does not replace human responsibility. Where AI affects people, we put their interests ahead of efficiency claims, cost savings, or technical novelty.*

**Assessment criteria (for evaluating company-submitted language against the foundation):**

- *Strong:* The company's submitted language places human interests substantively first in AI deployment decisions, can function as a tiebreaker when interests conflict, and is at least as strong as the foundation in its commitment. Examples of strong framings: "human well-being and rights take precedence over AI-driven efficiency gains in any deployment decision affecting people"; "AI deployment that fails to demonstrably support human work in our company will not be deployed regardless of cost savings."
- *Can be strengthened:* The company's language references human-centered values but in language vague enough that it could be interpreted as one consideration among many rather than a tiebreaker. Specific suggested strengthenings: name the interests AI deployment must not subordinate (worker interests, customer interests, community interests); make explicit that the principle operates as a decision criterion, not just a value statement.
- *Conflicts with human rights or binding law:* The company's submitted language inverts or undermines the people-first principle in a way that is incompatible with UNGPs Foundational Principle 11 or with binding HRDD obligations. Examples that would be flagged: "AI is our priority, human considerations follow"; "we deploy AI for productivity and address human impact where business needs allow"; "AI capabilities determine the boundary of human work in our organization." These framings cannot pass through; the Diagnostic produces a specific explanation and the foundation language is substituted with a recommendation that the company revisit the principle.

**Assessment criteria (when no company language is submitted):** Default — Diagnostic confirms the foundation language is present in the policy. *Covered:* foundation language is included verbatim or substantively. *Missing:* the policy does not include the foundation principle.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company submitted their own principle in the company-input field, the Generator integrates that language as the basis for the requirement (after Diagnostic evaluation against the conflicts threshold). (2) If the company has uploaded a Code of Conduct, sustainability policy, or other foundational document containing values statements about technology, work, or human-centered approach, the Generator extracts that voice and integrates it. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces the foundation language for the people-first principle as default.*

### Requirement 1.2 — Human accountability principle: every AI-supported decision affecting a person has a human who is accountable

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to ensuring that every AI-supported decision that materially affects a person has an identifiable human who is accountable for that decision. AI does not make decisions alone about people. Where AI produces outputs that influence decisions on hiring, performance, scheduling, termination, customer access, supplier engagement, or other matters affecting individuals, a named human reviews, approves where required, and bears responsibility.

**Tier:** 1b for high-risk AI systems where AI Act Article 14 applies; Tier 2 for established best practice in non-high-risk contexts; Tier 3 (framework principle) for the broader extension to non-high-risk contexts.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier. Legal floor for high-risk AI systems is set by AI Act Article 14 and applies to all deployers of such systems.

**Jurisdiction:** EU AI Act Article 14; GDPR Article 22; EU Platform Work Directive Article 7.

**Legal logic:** AI Act-derived (Article 14 human oversight) plus GDPR-derived (Article 22), with framework-original extension.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Article 14.
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR), Article 22.
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/2831 (Platform Work Directive), Article 7.
- *Interpretive:* Council of Europe Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence (2024), Article 14.

**Company input.** *If you have already established or are currently developing a position on human accountability for AI-supported decisions in your organization, please share it here. We will evaluate it against the foundation principle and against binding obligations under the AI Act and GDPR.*

[FIELD: Your principle on human accountability for AI-supported decisions, in your own words]

**Foundation language:** *Human accountability. Every AI-supported decision that materially affects a person has a person who is accountable for it. AI does not make decisions alone. Where AI produces outputs that influence decisions about people, a named human reviews, approves, and bears responsibility.*

**Assessment criteria (for evaluating company-submitted language):**

- *Strong:* The company's language affirms human accountability for AI-supported decisions, names the categories of decisions where the principle applies (hiring, performance, customer access, supplier engagement, etc.), and gives operational meaning to "accountability" beyond mere supervision (review, override authority, signature responsibility, consequences for the human accountable).
- *Can be strengthened:* The company's language affirms human oversight but is limited to AI Act high-risk systems strictly, treats accountability as supervision rather than ownership, or does not name the categories of decisions covered. Specific suggested strengthenings: extend the principle to cover decisions beyond AI Act high-risk; clarify that accountability includes consequences, not just review.
- *Conflicts with human rights or binding law:* The company's language permits AI to make decisions about people without human accountability where binding law requires it (AI Act Article 14 for high-risk; GDPR Article 22 for solely automated decisions producing legal or significant effects). Examples that would be flagged: "AI systems can make hiring decisions independently in our recruitment process"; "automated performance evaluations operate without human review for efficiency"; "AI-driven customer eligibility decisions are final without human appeal." These cannot pass through.

**Assessment criteria (when no company language is submitted):** *Covered:* foundation language is included. *Missing:* the policy does not include the foundation principle.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company submitted their own principle, the Generator integrates that language (after Diagnostic evaluation). (2) If the company has uploaded HR procedures, governance documents, or AI-related materials containing accountability statements, the Generator extracts and integrates relevant voice. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces the foundation language for the human accountability principle, including reference to AI Act Article 14 and GDPR Article 22 where applicable to the company's profile.*

### Requirement 1.3 — Transparency principle: people affected by AI have a right to know

**Statement.** The policy commits the company to transparency about AI use to the people affected by it. This includes workers (who know when AI is involved in decisions about their work), customers (who know when they are interacting with AI), suppliers (who know what the company expects of them on AI), and the public (who can find out what AI the company uses, in appropriate detail, where reporting obligations or voluntary disclosure standards apply).

**Tier:** 1b for transparency obligations under binding law; Tier 2 for broader best-practice transparency expectations; Tier 3 for the framework's framing of transparency as a precondition rather than an option.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** EU AI Act Articles 26(11), 50; GDPR Articles 13, 14, 22; EU Platform Work Directive Article 9; CSRD/ESRS where applicable.

**Legal logic:** Both AI Act-derived and HRDD-derived (UNGPs Principle 21).

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Articles 26(11), 50.
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR), Articles 13, 14, 22.
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/2831 (Platform Work Directive), Article 9.
- *Interpretive:* UNGPs Principle 21.

**Company input.** *If your organization has already developed a position on transparency about AI use — whether toward workers, customers, suppliers, or the public — please share it here. We will evaluate it against the foundation principle and against binding transparency obligations under the AI Act, GDPR, and applicable HRDD law.*

[FIELD: Your principle on transparency about AI use, in your own words]

**Foundation language:** *Transparency. People affected by our AI use have a right to know. Workers know when AI is involved in decisions about their work. Customers know when they are interacting with AI. Suppliers know what we expect of them on AI. The public knows what AI we use, in broad terms, where reporting obligations apply.*

**Assessment criteria (for evaluating company-submitted language):**

- *Strong:* The company's language commits to transparency at multiple audience levels (workers, customers, suppliers, public where applicable), is specific about what is disclosed at each level, and treats transparency as foundational rather than optional. Strong framings address the *operational consequence* of transparency — that without it, accountability and consultation cannot function.
- *Can be strengthened:* The company's language commits to transparency but addresses only one audience (most often customers via Article 50 obligations alone), commits without specifying what flows to which audience, or treats transparency as a marketing concept rather than a policy commitment. Specific suggested strengthenings: extend transparency to all four audiences; specify what is disclosed at each level.
- *Conflicts with human rights or binding law:* The company's language explicitly reserves the right to deploy AI without notification where binding law requires notification. Examples that would be flagged: "AI use in employment decisions does not require worker notification" (conflicts with AI Act Article 26(11) and GDPR Article 13–14); "automated content generation does not require disclosure to customers" (conflicts with AI Act Article 50). These cannot pass through.

**Assessment criteria (when no company language is submitted):** *Covered:* foundation language is included. *Missing:* the policy does not include the foundation principle.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company submitted their own principle, the Generator integrates it (after Diagnostic evaluation). (2) If the company has uploaded sustainability reporting, public disclosures, or transparency-related policies, the Generator extracts and integrates that voice. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces the foundation language addressing all four transparency audiences (workers, customers, suppliers, public).*

### Requirement 1.4 — Continuity principle: this policy extends existing values, it does not stand apart

**Statement.** The policy commits to coherence with the company's existing values, commitments, and policy infrastructure. The Human-AI policy extends — rather than parallels or replaces — the company's Code of Conduct, human rights commitments, Own Workforce Policy, Information Security policy, Data Protection policy, Whistleblowing procedure, Grievance mechanisms, and other foundational documents.

**Tier:** 3 (framework principle).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** Framework-original. Aligned with the integration logic in CSDDD Article 7 and Norwegian Transparency Act § 4(a).

**Legal logic:** Framework-original; informed by HRDD-derived integration principles.

**Source citations:**
- *Interpretive:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 7.
- *Interpretive:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4(a).
- *Framework-original:* The "continuity" framing as a named principle is project-original.

**Company input.** *If you have already articulated how AI policy fits within your existing governance — your Code of Conduct, human rights commitments, People or Own Workforce Policy, IT and Information Security policies — please share it here. We will evaluate it against the foundation principle and against binding integration obligations under CSDDD and the Transparency Act.*

[FIELD: Your principle on how this policy relates to existing internal policies, in your own words]

**Foundation language:** *Continuity with our values. This policy does not stand apart from our other commitments. It extends our Code of Conduct, our human rights commitments, our commitments to our own workforce, and our existing approach to responsible business — to AI as a new dimension of work, not a separate domain.*

**Assessment criteria (for evaluating company-submitted language):**

- *Strong:* The company's language explicitly positions the Human-AI policy as an extension of existing governance, names specific internal policies it cross-references, and connects AI-related obligations to existing mechanisms (risk management, grievance, audit, reporting) rather than building parallel structures.
- *Can be strengthened:* The company's language mentions other policies in passing but does not establish clear connections, or sets up parallel AI-specific structures (separate AI governance committee disconnected from existing risk governance, separate AI grievance channel disconnected from whistleblowing) without justification. Specific suggested strengthenings: name the specific cross-referenced policies; explicitly connect AI obligations to existing mechanisms.
- *Conflicts with human rights or binding law:* The company's language treats the Human-AI policy as superseding existing human rights commitments where the existing commitments would otherwise apply. Example that would be flagged: "Where this AI policy provides different guidance from our human rights policy, this policy prevails." This would defeat the integration logic that makes HRDD operational and would conflict with CSDDD Article 7.

**Assessment criteria (when no company language is submitted):** *Covered:* foundation language is included. *Missing:* the policy does not include the foundation principle.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) If the company submitted their own principle, the Generator integrates it (after Diagnostic evaluation). (2) The Generator extracts policy names and cross-reference structure from the company's uploaded policies (Code of Conduct, Own Workforce Policy, IT Policy, etc.) to populate specific cross-references. (3) If no company material is available, the Generator produces the foundation language with placeholder cross-references the company can refine ("our Code of Conduct," "our Own Workforce Policy") rather than naming specific document titles.*

## How this section activates across company profiles

All four principles activate for all three pilots regardless of ambition tier or jurisdictional exposure.

**Profile A — Mid-size manufacturer with mature HRDD (~2,000 employees, EU/Norway operations, established policy infrastructure, subject to Transparency Act and forthcoming CSDDD):** All four principles activate. The continuity principle (1.4) is particularly material — companies in this profile have mature existing policy infrastructure and the Diagnostic, run against existing policies, is likely to find that elements of all four principles are implicit in the Code of Conduct already and just need explicit AI extension. If the company submits company-specific language for the principles, the Diagnostic evaluates it against the foundation; given mature human rights orientation in this profile, "strong" outcomes are likely.

**Profile B — Safety-critical operations company in group context (~300 employees, Norwegian operations, parent group governance, HSE-led culture, exploratory AI deployment):** All four principles activate. The transparency principle (1.3) interacts with HSE-led culture in a useful way — physical safety transparency is already strong, but transparency about algorithmic processes affecting workers is new territory. If the company submits company-specific language drawn from the group framework, the Diagnostic evaluates it against the foundation.

**Profile C — Small specialty distributor below regulatory thresholds (~60 employees, lighter policy infrastructure, not currently bound by HRDD law):** All four principles activate. Because companies in this profile have lighter policy infrastructure, they may have less existing AI-related language to submit. The Generator's foundation language stands as the basis, and the company can refine over time as their AI thinking matures. The continuity principle (1.4) operates with what they have — even an employee handbook is something to extend, not duplicate.
