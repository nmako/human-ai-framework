## Section purpose

This section addresses how the company governs AI in its supply chain and value chain — both AI tools provided by suppliers that touch the company's operations, and AI tools used *by suppliers, on suppliers' own workers*, that constitute human rights risks in the company's value chain.

The second category is where this framework departs from most AI governance templates. The EU AI Act stops at the contracting boundary — it places obligations on providers, deployers, importers, and distributors but does not reach into how a supplier uses AI on its own workforce. Human rights due diligence regimes (UNGPs, Norwegian Transparency Act, EU CSDDD, UK Modern Slavery Act, German Lieferkettengesetz) operate on a different logic: they require the company to identify, prevent, mitigate, and remediate adverse impacts on people across its value chain, including impacts caused by suppliers. AI use by suppliers — algorithmic management, productivity monitoring, AI-driven scheduling, AI recruitment screening — is a category of adverse human rights impact that current corporate due diligence rarely captures.

This section operationalizes the integration: the company applies its existing HRDD machinery to AI as a category of supply chain risk, alongside the more familiar AI Act-derived obligations about supplier-provided AI tools.

## Generator behavior for this section

This section is heavily prescriptive (substance is determined by binding HRDD law) but Generator behavior is consistent across all eleven requirements. Section 8 operationalizes the supply chain dimension of the HRDD framework set out in Section 7 — the Generator ensures Section 8 outputs are consistent with Section 7's broader HRDD methodology rather than producing parallel substance. The Generator follows the cascading priority:

**Priority 1 — Company-specific HRDD framework.** If the company has uploaded a Transparency Act report (Norwegian åpenhetsloven § 5 report), CSDDD due diligence document, modern slavery statement, supplier code of conduct, or other HRDD-related materials, the Generator extracts the company's existing HRDD vocabulary, methodology references, and operational structure. Specific extraction targets: how the company refers to its own due diligence process (aktsomhetsvurderinger, due diligence assessment, supply chain assessment), what suppliers the company considers in its scope (Tier 1, Tier 2, multi-tier), what salience methodology the company uses, what remediation pathways the company has established, what reporting cycles operate.

**Priority 2 — Adjacent company materials.** If the company has uploaded a sustainability report, Code of Conduct, or supplier-facing materials with HRDD-adjacent language, the Generator draws on that voice to ensure consistency.

**Priority 3 — Foundation language with jurisdictional tailoring.** If no company HRDD material is available, the Generator produces the foundation language for each requirement with jurisdictional tailoring based on the configuration profile. For Norwegian companies the default reference is åpenhetsloven; for German companies LkSG; for French companies Loi de Vigilance; for in-CSDDD-scope companies the relevant CSDDD article. The framework's defining contribution — treating supplier AI use as a category of HRDD risk (per Section 7 Requirement 7.1) — is preserved verbatim regardless of company-specific tailoring.

For the trade controls requirement (8.11), the Generator additionally extracts company-specific trade flow information from uploaded supplier lists, sourcing documents, or import/export records to identify whether UFLPA, Mexico FLF, or similar instruments apply. If no such material is available, the Generator produces foundation language with a flag for the company to confirm trade flow exposure.

For the worker consultation requirement (8.10), Generator behavior follows the same cascade as Section 3 Requirement 3.5 — extracting collective agreement and consultation framework references where available, defaulting to applicable national codetermination law.

For the broader HRDD integration consistency, Generator behavior aligns Section 8 outputs with Section 7 outputs — the supply chain HRDD work uses the same UNGPs methodology vocabulary as the broader HRDD work, references the same jurisdictional HRDD law, and integrates with the same reporting cycles.

## Requirements

### Cluster 1: Identification — knowing where AI is in your supply chain

#### Requirement 8.1 — Inventory of supplier-provided AI tools

**Statement.** The company maintains, or commits to develop within a stated timeframe, an inventory of AI tools provided by suppliers that are used in the company's operations or that process the company's data.

**Tier:** 1b (procedural and disclosure obligation under EU AI Act for high-risk AI systems specifically; broader inventory expectation as Tier 2 best practice for non-high-risk AI)

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier. Companies cannot govern AI tools they do not know exist.

**Jurisdiction:** EU AI Act Art. 26 (deployer obligations for high-risk AI systems include knowing what high-risk AI systems are in use); broader inventory expectations from authoritative interpretive guidance (OECD AI Principles, UNGPs Principle 18 on identifying impacts).

**Legal logic:** AI Act-derived (the inventory is the foundation of any deployer obligation discharge).

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Article 26 — deployer obligations including use according to instructions and oversight (deployer must know which high-risk AI systems they use).
- *Interpretive:* OECD AI Principles, Principle 1.4 (transparency and explainability); UNGPs Principle 18 (assessing actual and potential human rights impacts requires knowing where impacts may occur).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to maintaining (or developing) an inventory of AI tools from suppliers, with at minimum: tool identification, supplier identification, function or business purpose, data processed, and risk classification (high-risk under AI Act / not high-risk).

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy states the company maintains or will develop an inventory of supplier-provided AI tools, including a stated timeframe if not yet in place, and identifies the minimum data fields the inventory captures.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions awareness of supplier AI but does not commit to a structured inventory, or commits without specifying scope or timeframe.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on knowing what supplier-provided AI tools the company uses.

#### Requirement 8.2 — Identification of AI use by suppliers affecting their own workers

**Statement.** The company commits to identifying, within its existing human rights due diligence process, where suppliers use AI in ways that affect workers in the supply chain — including algorithmic management, productivity monitoring, AI-driven scheduling, performance evaluation, recruitment, and termination decisions.

**Tier:** 1c (duty of care under HRDD regimes — Transparency Act § 4–5 requires identification of actual and potential adverse impacts on fundamental human rights and decent work; CSDDD Article 8 requires identification of adverse impacts in chain of activities). Activates at Tier 1c only when the company is bound by an HRDD regime; activates at Tier 2 (best practice under UNGPs methodology) for companies not currently bound but operating HRDD voluntarily.

**Scope:** Tier B and Tier C ambition; for companies bound by HRDD regimes the scope expands to include supply chain regardless of ambition tier selection.

**Jurisdiction:** Norwegian Transparency Act § 4–5; EU CSDDD Articles 5, 8 (transposition deadline 26 July 2027 per Directive 2025/794, application from 26 July 2028 phased by company size); German Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG) §§ 4–5 (currently with suspended reporting obligations pending CSDDD application; replacement legislation under preparation); French Loi n° 2017-399 relative au devoir de vigilance, Article L. 225-102-4 of the French Commercial Code (5,000 employees in France or 10,000 worldwide threshold; civil liability regime); Dutch Wet zorgplicht kinderarbeid (child labour due diligence; in force but adoption pending) and forthcoming Wet verantwoord en duurzaam internationaal ondernemen (broader HRDD bill, parliamentary process); UK Independent Anti-Slavery Commissioner's proposed model law (16 December 2025) combining forced labour import ban with corporate due diligence and civil/criminal liability — would replace the current MSA § 54 reporting regime if adopted.

Sectoral and narrower-scope HRDD instruments are noted here for cross-reference but do not impose the broader due diligence duty this requirement addresses: Swiss Code of Obligations Art. 964j-l + DDTrO Ordinance (limited to conflict minerals — tin, tantalum, tungsten, gold — and child labour); Dutch Wet zorgplicht kinderarbeid in its current form (limited to child labour due diligence).

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived. This is the framework's defining contribution — applying impact-based, value-chain-wide due diligence to AI as a category of risk.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven, 2021), § 4 (due diligence) and § 5 (information disclosure).
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Articles 5 (due diligence), 8 (identifying and assessing actual and potential adverse impacts), and 9 (prioritisation). Transposition deadline 26 July 2027; application from 26 July 2028 (Directive (EU) 2025/794 amendment).
- *Binding:* Lieferkettensorgfaltspflichtengesetz (LkSG, Germany 2021), §§ 4–5 (risk management and risk analysis). Note: reporting obligations suspended pending CSDDD application; replacement legislation in preparation.
- *Binding:* Loi n° 2017-399 relative au devoir de vigilance des sociétés mères et des entreprises donneuses d'ordre (France, 2017), Code de commerce Article L. 225-102-4. Includes civil liability.
- *Binding (sectoral, where applicable):* Swiss Code of Obligations Articles 964j-l and DDTrO Ordinance (conflict minerals and child labour only); Dutch Wet zorgplicht kinderarbeid (child labour only).
- *Forthcoming:* Dutch Wet verantwoord en duurzaam internationaal ondernemen (broader HRDD bill); UK Independent Anti-Slavery Commissioner's proposed model law (December 2025).
- *Binding:* UK Modern Slavery Act 2015, § 54 (reporting-only — substantive due diligence covered in this requirement is informed by but not directly mandated by MSA § 54; see Requirement 8.9 for the reporting dimension).
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (2011), Principles 17–21 (operational principles on human rights due diligence). OHCHR B-Tech Project on AI and human rights.

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to including AI use by suppliers as a category considered in the company's human rights due diligence process. The commitment should be specific enough to be actionable — naming the categories of supplier AI use the company considers (algorithmic management, productivity monitoring, recruitment, scheduling, performance evaluation), and stating that these are integrated into the company's existing aktsomhetsvurderinger or equivalent HRDD process.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy explicitly names supplier AI use as a category considered in the company's HRDD process, identifies the types of supplier AI use considered, and states that AI-related findings are integrated into the same remediation and reporting processes as other HRDD findings.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions supplier AI in general terms but does not integrate it into the existing HRDD process, or does so without specifying which categories of supplier AI use are considered.
- *Missing:* The policy treats supplier AI as an IT or procurement issue only, with no reference to human rights due diligence.

#### Requirement 8.3 — Risk prioritization in supply chain AI assessment

**Statement.** The company prioritizes its supply chain AI risk assessment based on salience — the severity, scope, and likelihood of adverse impacts on workers and other affected populations — rather than on supplier size, contract value, or geographic proximity alone.

**Tier:** 1c when bound by HRDD regime that requires risk-based prioritization (CSDDD, Transparency Act); Tier 2 (UNGPs methodology) otherwise.

**Scope:** Tier B and Tier C ambition; legally floored to Tier B when HRDD regime applies.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 9 (prioritization based on severity and likelihood); UNGPs Principle 24 (where it is necessary to prioritize, address most severe impacts first); OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 9 (prioritization where addressing all impacts simultaneously is not feasible).
- *Interpretive:* UNGPs Principle 24; OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct (2018), particularly the section on prioritization.

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to a risk-based approach to supplier AI assessment, prioritizing salient impacts (severity × scope × likelihood) rather than defaulting to commercial criteria. It should acknowledge that this means a small high-risk supplier may receive more attention than a large low-risk one.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to salience-based prioritization, defines salience or references the UNGPs / OECD methodology, and states this approach applies to AI-related supply chain risks.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to risk-based prioritization but does not define salience or distinguishes only by commercial criteria.
- *Missing:* The policy treats all suppliers identically or prioritizes based on contract value alone.

### Cluster 2: Contractual and procurement requirements

#### Requirement 8.4 — Supplier disclosure expectations regarding AI

**Statement.** The company sets disclosure expectations for suppliers regarding AI use that affects shared operations, the company's data, or supplier workers. These expectations are documented in supplier codes of conduct, master service agreements, or equivalent contractual mechanisms.

**Tier:** 1b for high-risk AI systems used by suppliers and provided to the company (AI Act deployer obligations require knowing what is being deployed); Tier 2 broader supplier disclosure expectations as best practice.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier. Even Tier A ambition companies must know what AI tools they are using.

**Jurisdiction:** EU AI Act Article 26 and Article 13 (instructions for use require provider-supplied information); supplier disclosure as best practice under OECD Guidelines for MNEs Chapter II (general policies).

**Legal logic:** AI Act-derived primarily; HRDD-derived for the supplier-on-worker disclosure dimension.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), Articles 13, 26.
- *Interpretive:* OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (2023 update), Chapter II on general policies; OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct.

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to including AI-specific disclosure expectations in supplier-facing instruments. At minimum: requiring suppliers to disclose AI tools they provide to the company; requiring disclosure of AI used by the supplier in ways that affect the supplier's own workers (where the framework's ambition reaches Tier B or higher); and requiring notification when a supplier introduces new AI use that affects shared operations.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy explicitly commits to AI disclosure expectations in supplier contracts or codes of conduct, names what suppliers must disclose, and states the mechanism (contracts, supplier code of conduct, audits) used to operationalize the expectation.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions supplier expectations on AI but does not specify what is disclosed or how the expectation is operationalized.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on supplier disclosure regarding AI.

#### Requirement 8.5 — Proportionate supplier expectations based on capability and resources

**Statement.** The company sets supplier expectations on AI governance proportionate to supplier capability, sector, and the materiality of AI use in the supplier relationship. Where supplier capability is limited, the company commits to capability-building support rather than imposing unrealistic demands.

**Tier:** 2 (established best practice under OECD Guidelines and UNGPs); Tier 3 (framework principle) where the commitment to capability-building is concerned — this is where the framework goes beyond current standard practice.

**Scope:** Tier B and Tier C.

**Jurisdiction:** UNGPs Principle 19 (taking appropriate action depends on the company's leverage, severity of abuse, complicity); OECD Due Diligence Guidance.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (2011), Principle 19 and commentary on leverage and supplier relationships.
- *Interpretive:* OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct (2018), particularly on responsible disengagement and capability-building.

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should acknowledge that supplier capability varies and commit to a proportionate approach: stronger expectations on suppliers with greater capability and where AI use is material; capability-building support for suppliers with limited resources; and clear escalation paths where a supplier is unable or unwilling to meet expectations on material AI risks.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy explicitly addresses supplier capability differences, commits to proportionate expectations and capability-building, and identifies escalation logic for cases where proportionate engagement is insufficient.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions proportionality but does not commit to capability-building support, or commits to capability-building without escalation logic.
- *Missing:* The policy treats supplier expectations uniformly, with no acknowledgement of capability differences or capability-building support.

### Cluster 3: Monitoring, remediation, and ongoing relationship

#### Requirement 8.6 — Monitoring AI-related supplier impacts during the relationship

**Statement.** The company monitors suppliers for AI-related adverse impacts on workers throughout the supplier relationship, integrated with existing supplier monitoring mechanisms (audits, supplier reviews, grievance channels, worker voice mechanisms).

**Tier:** 1c when bound by HRDD regime requiring ongoing tracking of impacts (CSDDD Article 15); Tier 2 otherwise.

**Scope:** Tier B and Tier C ambition; legally floored to Tier B when HRDD regime applies.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 15 (monitoring of due diligence effectiveness); Norwegian Transparency Act § 4 (continuous due diligence); UNGPs Principle 20 (tracking effectiveness of response).

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 15 (monitoring of effectiveness of due diligence policy and measures, with periodic assessments at least every 12 months).
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4 — due diligence as a continuous process.
- *Interpretive:* UNGPs Principle 20.

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to ongoing monitoring of AI-related supplier impacts, integrated with existing monitoring mechanisms rather than as a parallel process. It should reference how AI-specific findings are surfaced in audits, supplier reviews, or other existing channels.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to ongoing monitoring of supplier AI impacts, names the existing mechanisms through which monitoring happens, and states how AI-specific findings are integrated into existing supplier review and audit processes.
- *Partial:* The policy commits to monitoring but does not specify mechanisms or integration points, or treats AI monitoring as a separate parallel process from existing supplier monitoring.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on ongoing monitoring of supplier AI use.

#### Requirement 8.7 — Remediation when supplier AI causes adverse impacts on workers

**Statement.** When AI use by a supplier causes or contributes to adverse impacts on workers, the company commits to engaging with the supplier to identify, prevent, mitigate, or remediate the impact, with access to grievance and remedy mechanisms appropriate to the severity of the impact.

**Tier:** 1c (CSDDD Article 11 obligations on bringing actual adverse impacts to an end and Article 12 on remediation; Transparency Act § 4 obligation on remediation).

**Scope:** Tier B and Tier C ambition; legally floored to Tier B when HRDD regime applies.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Articles 11, 12; Norwegian Transparency Act § 4; UNGPs Principles 22, 29, 31.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Articles 11 (bringing actual adverse impacts to an end) and 12 (remediation of actual adverse impacts).
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4.
- *Interpretive:* UNGPs Principle 22 (remediation), 29 (operational-level grievance mechanisms), 31 (effectiveness criteria for non-judicial grievance mechanisms).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to a remediation pathway for supplier AI-related adverse impacts: engagement with the supplier, where possible joint remediation, and access to grievance mechanisms for affected workers. It should reference the company's existing grievance mechanism (cross-referencing the company's whistleblowing channel or human rights grievance channel — see Section 10 of the rubric).

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to remediation for supplier AI-related adverse impacts, identifies the engagement and remediation approach, and provides clear cross-reference to grievance mechanisms accessible to affected workers (including supplier workers).
- *Partial:* The policy commits to remediation but does not specify the approach, or references grievance mechanisms without confirming they are accessible to supplier workers.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on remediation for supplier AI-related adverse impacts.

### Cluster 4: Integration with existing HRDD

#### Requirement 8.8 — Integration with existing supply chain due diligence

**Statement.** The company integrates AI-related supply chain risk assessment into its existing supply chain human rights due diligence rather than operating a parallel process. AI is treated as a category of risk within the existing aktsomhetsvurderinger or equivalent HRDD framework.

**Tier:** 1c when bound by HRDD regime; Tier 2 otherwise. The integration is the framework's design principle — adding AI to existing infrastructure rather than building parallel structures.

**Scope:** Tier B and Tier C ambition; legally floored where applicable.

**Jurisdiction:** Norwegian Transparency Act § 4 (continuous due diligence); EU CSDDD Articles 7–11 (integrated due diligence); German Lieferkettengesetz § 4; UNGPs Principle 17 (integrated due diligence).

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Lov om virksomheters åpenhet og arbeid med grunnleggende menneskerettigheter og anstendige arbeidsforhold (åpenhetsloven), § 4.
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Articles 7–11.
- *Interpretive:* UNGPs Principle 17 and commentary; OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct (2018).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should explicitly state that AI-related supply chain risks are integrated into the company's existing HRDD process, not addressed in a separate AI-specific track. The cross-reference to the company's HRDD process should be specific (naming the process or document) rather than generic.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy explicitly cross-references the company's existing HRDD process by name (e.g., "our aktsomhetsvurderinger conducted under the Transparency Act") and confirms AI risks are integrated into that process.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions integration but the cross-reference is generic, or describes both an AI-specific process and HRDD process without confirming they are the same process.
- *Missing:* The policy treats AI as a separate track from supply chain HRDD.

**Source citations:**

- *Binding:* UK Modern Slavery Act 2015, § 54. Note: under review per UK Trade Strategy 2025 and may be replaced by the Independent Anti-Slavery Commissioner's December 2025 proposed model law combining import ban with corporate due diligence.
- *Binding:* Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Australia, Cth).

- *Binding:* California Transparency in Supply Chains Act (SB 657, 2010).

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy explicitly commits to addressing AI in the company's HRDD reporting under specific named obligations, and confirms integration with sustainability reporting where relevant (CSRD/ESRS).

- *Missing:* The policy is silent on AI in mandatory HRDD reporting.

#### Requirement 8.10 — Worker consultation on supply chain AI matters

**Statement.** Where supply chain AI use materially affects workers — including own workforce in shared operations, supplier workers, or workers in joint ventures — the company commits to meaningful consultation with worker representatives (works councils, unions, employee representatives) in line with applicable codetermination frameworks and existing collective agreements.

**Tier:** 1c (CSDDD Article 13 stakeholder engagement; national codetermination law where applicable). For Norwegian companies operating under collective agreements, this also engages national consultation frameworks.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier where workers are materially affected. The legal floor is Tier B when CSDDD or codetermination law applies.

**Jurisdiction:** EU CSDDD Article 13 (meaningful engagement with stakeholders); EU Platform Work Directive (2024/2831), particularly on algorithmic management transparency; Norwegian Working Environment Act and collective agreement frameworks; German Betriebsverfassungsgesetz; UNGPs Principle 18(b) on stakeholder engagement.

**Legal logic:** Both AI Act-derived (Platform Work Directive intersection) and HRDD-derived (CSDDD stakeholder engagement, UNGPs).

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/1760 (CSDDD), Article 13.
- *Binding:* Directive (EU) 2024/2831 (Platform Work Directive), particularly Articles 7–10 on algorithmic management.
- *Binding:* Lov om arbeidsmiljø, arbeidstid og stillingsvern mv. (Arbeidsmiljøloven, Norway).
- *Interpretive:* UNGPs Principle 18(b); OECD Guidelines for MNEs Chapter V (employment and industrial relations).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to consultation with worker representatives on supply chain AI matters that materially affect workers. The commitment should reference applicable collective agreements or works council frameworks where they exist, and acknowledge the cross-cutting nature of worker consultation (the requirement appears in multiple sections of the rubric, not only here).

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy commits to meaningful worker consultation on supply chain AI matters materially affecting workers, references applicable codetermination or collective agreement frameworks, and aligns with the cross-cutting worker consultation requirements elsewhere in the policy.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions consultation but does not specify the framework, or limits consultation to own workforce without addressing supplier workers in shared operations.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on worker consultation regarding supply chain AI.

#### Requirement 8.11 — Trade controls and import-ban-driven due diligence on AI-related forced labour risks

**Statement.** Where the company imports or trades in goods that may be subject to forced labour import bans or rebuttable-presumption regimes, the company conducts heightened supply chain due diligence to verify that goods sourced from designated regions or sectors are not produced with forced labour, including AI-supported forced labour management. Trade controls of this kind impose de facto due diligence obligations even where no formal HRDD duty exists in the company's home jurisdiction.

**Tier:** 1a (prohibitions and hard limits — these are import bans, not procedural obligations; non-compliance means goods cannot lawfully enter the relevant market).

**Scope:** All — applies to any company importing into a jurisdiction with active forced labour controls, regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** US Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA, 2022) — rebuttable presumption that goods sourced wholly or in part from Xinjiang are made with forced labour; Mexico Federal Law on Forced Labour (2023) — import prohibition following the US model; Brazil Lei nº 14.946/2024 (Lei Beneditto) — adds forced labour prevention requirements to public procurement; proposed UK forced labour import ban (per Independent Anti-Slavery Commissioner's December 2025 model law); proposed Canadian Bill C-251 (rebuttable presumption similar to UFLPA, under consideration).

**Legal logic:** Hybrid. The legal instrument is a trade control (customs / import enforcement), but the operational effect on the company is heightened due diligence — companies must produce evidence that imported goods are not forced-labour-tainted, which functions identically to a due diligence obligation. AI-related dimension: where forced labour at source involves algorithmic management, AI surveillance of detained or coerced workers, or AI-driven productivity coercion, the company's due diligence must address these specifically.

**Source citations:**
- *Binding:* Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act, 22 U.S.C. § 6901 note (2022).
- *Binding:* Mexico Federal Law on Forced Labour (2023).
- *Binding:* Brazil Lei nº 14.946/2024 (Lei Beneditto).
- *Forthcoming:* UK Independent Anti-Slavery Commissioner's proposed model law (December 2025); Canadian Bill C-251 (under consideration).
- *Interpretive:* US Customs and Border Protection (CBP) guidance on UFLPA enforcement and rebuttable presumption practice.

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should commit to identifying supply chain exposure to jurisdictions or sectors subject to forced labour import bans, conducting heightened due diligence on those flows, and where AI-related forced labour indicators are present (algorithmic management of detained workers, AI surveillance, AI-driven productivity coercion), addressing them specifically. The policy should also commit to evidentiary documentation sufficient to rebut presumptions where goods are not in fact forced-labour-tainted.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy explicitly addresses forced labour import bans applicable to the company's trade flows, names heightened due diligence procedures for affected flows, addresses AI-related forced labour indicators, and confirms evidentiary documentation practices.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions forced labour generally but does not address import-ban-driven heightened due diligence, or addresses heightened due diligence without addressing AI-related forced labour indicators.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on forced labour import bans despite material trade flows into affected jurisdictions.

## How this section activates across company profiles

To stress-test the section, here is how requirements activate for each of the three pilots:

**Profile A — Mid-size manufacturer with mature HRDD (Tier B ambition, bound by Transparency Act + CSDDD from 2028 phased application):**
All eleven requirements activate. Tier 1c requirements (8.2, 8.3, 8.6, 8.7, 8.8, 8.10) activate at full force. Reporting requirement 8.9 activates because companies in this profile are subject to Transparency Act § 5 reporting now and will activate further obligations as CSDDD and CSRD/ESRS S2 reporting come online. Requirement 8.11 (trade controls) activates where the company has US operations or sources components from regions potentially associated with forced labour — UFLPA exposure may be material if any supply chain components originate in or transit through implicated regions. The framework's defining contribution — applying HRDD logic to AI in the value chain — gets full expression here.

**Profile B — Safety-critical operations company in group context (Tier B ambition, bound by Transparency Act):**
Ten of eleven requirements activate. CSDDD-specific elements within multi-source requirements cite Transparency Act as the binding source. Requirement 8.9 activates with respect to Transparency Act § 5 reporting only. Requirement 8.11 (trade controls) likely deactivates for companies whose operations are domestic and do not involve material import flows from forced-labour-implicated regions. Group context informs but does not formally cascade — group-context handling is a v1.1 development area.

**Profile C — Small specialty distributor below regulatory thresholds (likely Tier A or Tier B ambition, not bound by HRDD regimes):**
Tier 1c requirements deactivate (no HRDD regime currently binds companies below regulatory thresholds). They surface in the diagnostic output as "Available to align with voluntarily" rather than as gaps. Tier 1b and Tier 2 requirements (notably 8.1 inventory and 8.4 supplier disclosure) remain activated. Requirement 8.11 may activate depending on import flows — specialty importers may handle products from regions subject to attention under forced labour controls (cocoa, certain seafood, certain agricultural products), and the questionnaire would surface this. The honest output for this profile is: *"You are not currently bound to extend HRDD to AI in your supply chain, but the framework recommends voluntary alignment because national regulation is moving in this direction, customer relationships increasingly expect this from suppliers, and specific trade controls may apply to your import flows regardless of broader HRDD obligations."*

This activation pattern is what the configuration model promises: requirements deactivate honestly when not applicable, surface as voluntary alignment opportunities, and never fabricate obligations the law does not impose.

**Platform Work Directive intersection in 8.10.** The Platform Work Directive (2024/2831) has specific provisions on algorithmic management that materially affect supplier worker situations, particularly in logistics and platform-mediated work. Whether this belongs in supply chain (8.10) or in worker rights and human oversight (Section 3) is a structural question. I've placed it here because the supply chain context is where it is most likely to surface in pilot organizations. Worth your view.

**Cross-cutting worker consultation requirement.** Requirement 8.10 is one instance of a cross-cutting requirement that probably appears in three or four sections (Worker rights, Risk assessment, Supply chain, Grievance mechanisms). Worth deciding whether to handle this through repeated requirements (with explicit cross-references), through a separate cross-cutting requirements file, or through a tag system that flags worker-consultation-relevant requirements across the rubric. The configuration model already supports the tag approach; question is whether the rubric document itself uses tags or repeats the requirement.

**Length and density.** This section runs to eleven requirements with full assessment criteria. Across twelve sections, this would produce a rubric document of roughly 110 requirements and 35,000+ words. Worth checking whether this scale is what you want, or whether some sections should be lighter (5–7 requirements) to keep the overall document navigable. **Trade controls placement (8.11).** I placed trade controls as a separate requirement within the supply chain section because the operational logic is supply-chain-shaped (heightened due diligence on specific flows). An alternative would be to place it in a separate section on regulatory compliance overlays. Your view.

**Ambition above legal floor — does the example show this clearly enough?** The activation logic for the three pilots illustrates the legal floor / ambition ceiling distinction. Worth your view on whether this is the right way to demonstrate the architecture, or whether each requirement should carry a more explicit "legal floor: yes/no" tag in addition to the scope tag.
