## Section purpose

This section names the external frameworks — binding instruments and voluntary standards — that the Human-AI policy aligns with. It is the operational instantiation of accountability beyond the company itself: the policy is not a purely internal commitment but an alignment with established legal frameworks and global standards.

The section serves three functions. First, it establishes clear scope of what the policy commits to comply with — making binding obligations explicit to anyone reading the policy. Second, it establishes voluntary alignments that go beyond binding obligations — making the company's ambition visible. Third, it provides a stable reference point as external frameworks evolve — the policy commits to tracking changes and updating internal commitments accordingly.

The framework distinguishes two categories. Binding instruments are laws and regulations the company is legally subject to; non-compliance carries legal consequences. Voluntary frameworks are standards the company chooses to align with; alignment is a positive commitment but non-alignment does not carry direct legal consequences. The relationship between them: voluntary frameworks often inform interpretation of binding instruments (UNGPs is referenced in CSDDD; OECD Guidelines inform sector-specific regulation), and binding instruments often codify previously-voluntary frameworks (CSDDD operationalizes UNGPs for in-scope companies; AI Act codifies elements of the OECD AI Principles).

The framework's distinctive position in this section is that voluntary alignment is not optional accountability. Even where binding HRDD law does not apply to a company, the framework treats UNGPs methodology, OECD Guidelines, ILO Conventions, and similar global standards as expected baseline commitments. A company below the Norwegian Transparency Act threshold is not legally obligated to operate aktsomhetsvurderinger, but the framework's position is that such a company still aligns with UNGPs as the global standard for responsible business conduct. Voluntary alignment is the framework's way of extending accountability beyond legal compulsion.

The section is weighted light executive treatment because naming external frameworks is standard policy practice but does not carry substantive commitments. The substantive obligations live in the sections that transpose each framework. The executive commitment in this section provides scope orientation rather than substantive promise.

### Requirement 12.2 — Named voluntary frameworks the company aligns with

**Statement.** The policy names the voluntary frameworks the company aligns with as global standards going beyond binding minimum. The framework's position is that voluntary alignment is baseline expectation: certain global standards inform responsible AI use regardless of binding law applicability. The minimum voluntary alignment expected by the framework includes:

- **UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (2011)** — the universal methodology for human rights due diligence, including AI-related impacts on people.
- **OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (2023 update)** — the framework for responsible business conduct including responsible AI use.
- **Applicable ILO Conventions** — international labor standards relevant to AI in employment contexts.
- **Council of Europe Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence (2024)** — the international human rights framework for AI, entered into force on 1 September 2025.
- **ISO/IEC 42001:2023** — the international management system standard for AI (alignment is voluntary but increasingly expected for organizations operating substantial AI deployments).
- **UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence (2021)** — the global ethics standard for AI adopted by 193 member states.

The policy may name additional voluntary frameworks based on company context (sector-specific standards, industry coalitions, certification frameworks).

**Tier:** 2 (established best practice — these are the global standards that informed responsible business conduct expects). Tier 3 (framework principle) for the framework's position that voluntary alignment is baseline expectation rather than truly optional.

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** Global voluntary frameworks.

**Legal logic:** HRDD-derived primarily; informed by ethical AI literature.

**Source citations:**
- *Interpretive:* UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (2011).
- *Interpretive:* OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (2023 update).
- *Interpretive:* ILO Conventions, particularly the eight fundamental Conventions on freedom of association, forced labor, child labor, and discrimination.
- *Interpretive:* Council of Europe Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence and Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law (CETS No. 225, opened for signature 5 September 2024, entered into force 1 September 2025).
- *Interpretive:* ISO/IEC 42001:2023 — Artificial intelligence — Management system.
- *Interpretive:* UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence (adopted 23 November 2021).
- *Framework-original:* The position that voluntary alignment with these frameworks is baseline expectation rather than optional.

### Requirement 12.3 — Relationship between binding instruments and voluntary frameworks

**Statement.** The policy is explicit about how binding instruments and voluntary frameworks interact. The framework's position is that voluntary frameworks often inform interpretation of binding instruments (UNGPs methodology is referenced in CSDDD; OECD Guidelines inform sector-specific regulation; ISO/IEC 42001 informs AI Act conformity work). Binding instruments do not exhaust the company's commitments — voluntary alignment continues to operate as a separate commitment. The policy avoids treating compliance with binding minimums as sufficient.

**Tier:** 3 (framework principle).

**Scope:** All — applies regardless of ambition tier.

**Jurisdiction:** Framework-original.

**Legal logic:** Framework-original; informed by HRDD methodology integration logic.

**Source citations:**
- *Framework-original:* The position that binding compliance does not exhaust commitments.
- *Cross-reference:* Section 1 Requirement 1.1 (people first principle).

**What to look for in a policy.** The policy should address the binding/voluntary relationship explicitly, avoid framing compliance with binding minimums as sufficient, and clarify that voluntary frameworks operate as substantive commitments alongside binding compliance.

**Assessment criteria:**
- *Covered:* The policy addresses the binding/voluntary relationship, avoids minimum-compliance framing, and treats voluntary frameworks as substantive commitments.
- *Partial:* The policy mentions both binding and voluntary frameworks but does not address their relationship, or implicitly treats binding compliance as sufficient.
- *Missing:* The policy is silent on the relationship, or explicitly treats binding compliance as the ceiling of commitment.

**Generator behavior.** *(1) The Generator produces foundation language addressing the relationship, drawing on the company's voice from uploaded materials where available. (2) If no company material is available, the Generator produces standard foundation language naming the relationship and the framework's position on voluntary alignment as substantive commitment.*

## How this section activates across company profiles

**Company subject to mature regulatory exposure (EU operations, in-CSDDD-scope by 2029, Norwegian Transparency Act, GDPR, sector-specific regulation):** Requirement 12.1 activates substantively with a long list of binding instruments. Requirement 12.2 activates with baseline voluntary frameworks plus any company-specific additions. Requirement 12.3 activates substantively given the multiple binding/voluntary intersections. Requirement 12.4 activates with substantial tracking workload.

**Company subject to moderate regulatory exposure (Norwegian operations only, below CSDDD threshold, GDPR-bound, no sector-specific AI regulation):** Requirement 12.1 activates with a moderate list of binding instruments (åpenhetsloven, GDPR, AI Act as deployer). Requirements 12.2 through 12.4 activate at standard scope.

**Company subject to minimal regulatory exposure (small company below Transparency Act threshold, EU AI Act applies as deployer, GDPR for personal data processing):** Requirement 12.1 activates with the few binding instruments that apply. Requirement 12.2 activates substantively because voluntary alignment is the framework's commitment to accountability beyond binding minimum — UNGPs methodology applies to a 60-employee company even where Transparency Act does not. The diagnostic output makes this visible.

**Company subject to non-EU regulatory exposure (US operations, no CSDDD scope, no Transparency Act, but voluntary alignment with global standards):** Requirement 12.1 activates with US-specific instruments (state-level AI regulation, sector-specific federal regulation, applicable trade controls including UFLPA). Requirement 12.2 activates with global voluntary frameworks. The framework's position translates to a non-EU context: binding compliance with applicable US instruments alongside voluntary alignment with global standards.

This activation pattern reinforces the framework's position that voluntary alignment with global frameworks operates as baseline expectation regardless of binding regulatory exposure. The framework treats voluntary alignment as the path to accountability beyond legal compulsion.
